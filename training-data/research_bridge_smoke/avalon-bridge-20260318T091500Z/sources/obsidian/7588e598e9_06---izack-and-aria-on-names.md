# Izack and Aria on Names

The nursery had not been a nursery when Izack first built it.

It had been a calibration room.

That was what he had told Aria, at least, while pretending the cradle-sized alcove in the wall and the soft gold lantern-light had happened by accident. The chamber sat one floor below their quarters in a quieter bend of the Spire, where the crystal walls held heat well and the harmonic lines ran gentle instead of bright. At night the whole room breathed with a low, steady pulse, as if the tower had decided that if it could not sleep, it could at least learn how to be careful.

Alexander was asleep in the cradle near the window.

Or nearly asleep. With Alexander it was hard to tell. Even at rest he seemed to be conducting private negotiations with reality. One small hand was open above the blanket, fingers curling now and then as though he were reaching for runes that had not arrived yet.

Izack sat cross-legged on the floor beside the cradle with a notebook balanced on one knee and a mug of tea he had forgotten to drink cooling beside him. He had ink on the side of his hand, on one cuff, and somehow on his jaw. Aria had not asked how. She had been married to him long enough to understand that some questions wasted time.

She stood by the window, one hand resting against the crystal frame, watching Avalon drift below. The World Tree's leaves gave off their own faint silver light. Beyond them the floating bridges and garden terraces of the Academy shimmered in the dark like thoughts not yet spoken aloud.

"You are thinking too loudly," she said.

Izack looked up. "I wasn't aware that had become measurable."

"With you? It has always been measurable."

He smiled, tired and fond. "That sounds unfair."

"It sounds accurate."

He set the notebook down on the rug. "I was not thinking loudly. I was thinking responsibly."

"Dangerous distinction."

"Aria."

She turned from the window. There was amusement in her face now, quiet but unmistakable. "What are you naming?"

"Not naming," he said. "Considering."

"Worse."

He drew his legs in a little closer. "We have one son."

"I had noticed."

"And he has a proper name."

"Alexander Rune Thorne," she said. "Yes. I was present for that discussion as well."

"A very successful discussion," Izack said. "Elegant. Balanced. Historically resonant without sounding like he was born eighty years old."

Aria crossed the room and sat opposite him on the rug, folding one leg beneath herself. "You are trying to convince me of something."

"No."

"Izack."

He exhaled. "Yes."

She waited.

"I was thinking," he said carefully, "that if we are very fortunate, and somewhat brave, and the universe remains only moderately catastrophic, he may not be our only child."

The line of her mouth softened.

"Go on," she said.

His expression changed at once. Not into certainty. Into delight. That was always the danger with him. Give Izack one inch of emotional permission and he would immediately build a system around it.

"I was thinking," he repeated, "that names are a kind of architecture. The first structure you give a person. A shape they inhabit before they know how to build one for themselves."

"That," Aria said, "is the most you sentence possible."

"I know."

"And?"

He reached for the notebook and opened to a page crowded with notes, crossed-out words, and one diagram that looked suspiciously like he had tried to map phonetic resonance to leyline geometry.

Aria looked at it for three full seconds.

"You graphed baby names."

"Only provisionally."

"That is worse."

"In my defense, phonemes carry pattern weight."

"In your defense," Aria said, "you have none."

He laughed under his breath. Alexander shifted in the cradle but did not wake.

Izack lowered his voice. "All right. Listen. If we have daughters..."

"If."

"If," he conceded. "I have been thinking about names that sound like themselves. Not titles. Not banners. Not the sort of names noble families choose because they want the child to arrive already burdened by six generations of dead expectations."

"A noble criticism from a man whose son is named Alexander."

"Alexander is a good name."

"Alexander is a very royal name."

"Yes, but Rune keeps him honest."

That drew the smallest laugh from her.

"Fine," Aria said. "Tell me your dangerous thoughts."

Izack glanced toward the cradle, then back at her, and the whole room seemed to narrow to that small circle of lantern-light between them.

"Lyra," he said. "Lyra Starveil Thorne."

Aria went still in the way she did when something struck true before she was ready to admit it.

"Starveil," she repeated.

"Too much?"

"No," she said, after a moment. "Possibly exactly enough."

He brightened immediately. "Yes?"

"It sounds like a child who belongs to both sky and threshold," Aria said. "A name for someone who can look into distance without disappearing into it."

"That was roughly the theory."

"Roughly?"

"I did not have all the language for it."

"You had a graph."

"That was support material."

She shook her head, but there was no real censure in it.

"Lyra Starveil Thorne," Aria said again, testing the weight. "That would suit a girl who watched patterns before she spoke."

"Or laughed at them," Izack said.

"That too."

He turned the page.

"Mira," he said. "Mira Lightscribe Thorne."

Aria's brows lifted.

"Lightscribe?"

"I know how it sounds."

"Do you?"

"Yes. Slightly dramatic. But in a respectable scholarly direction."

She took the notebook from him and looked down at the page. In the margin he had written: *for a child who writes what the light remembers.*

Her expression changed.

"You are impossible," she said softly.

"That is not a no."

"No," she agreed. "It is not."

She handed the notebook back to him. "Mira Lightscribe Thorne sounds like someone who would read by windows before dawn and know more than she says."

"Exactly."

"You planned personalities for hypothetical children."

"Only lightly."

"Izack."

"Moderately."

Aria's mouth twitched.

"And the third?" she asked.

Now he looked almost guilty.

That was enough warning by itself.

"Tell me."

"You are going to object."

"Almost certainly."

"I would like the record to show that I know this."

"It may show whatever it likes. Speak."

Izack folded the notebook half shut as if that might somehow protect him.

"Senna," he said. "Senna Fizzle Thorne."

Aria stared at him.

Then she stared harder.

From somewhere high in the rafters came a dry clicking sound that might have been the Spire adjusting its crystal joints or might have been Polly silently laughing at him from a beam.

"Fizzle," Aria repeated.

"Yes."

"You want to give a royal child the middle name Fizzle."

"In honor of Fizzle Brightcog," he said quickly. "Which is different."

"Is it?"

"Substantially."

Aria pressed her fingers to her mouth, not because she was horrified but because she was losing the battle against amusement and wanted him to suffer for at least another second.

"Izack."

"She saved the west conduits twice," he said. "And the archive stabilizers. And that incident with the mirrored boilers was only arguably her fault."

"Arguably."

"More importantly," he said, leaning forward now, earnestness overtaking his embarrassment, "she built half the systems that keep this place running and never once asked for a statue, a title, or an inherited seat. If kings can name children after dead conquerors, I think we are allowed to name one after a woman who actually fixed things."

That took the laughter out of Aria's face and replaced it with thought.

"You are serious."

"I am."

She looked toward the window, toward the drifting lights of Avalon below. When she spoke again, her voice had gone quieter.

"Senna Fizzle Thorne," she said. "A child named for spark before flame. For ingenuity instead of bloodline. For someone who made function out of chaos."

"That was the thought."

"It is still an outrageous name."

"Yes."

"Court would hate it."

"Yes."

"My father would develop a facial expression so severe it would become architectural."

"Almost certainly."

She let that sit for a few seconds.

Then, very slowly, she smiled.

"Good," she said.

Izack blinked. "Good?"

"If a name cannot survive one offended council chamber, it is not strong enough for one of our children."

He laughed then, too startled to contain it, and Alexander made a soft sleepy sound in the cradle as if objecting to being excluded from the conversation.

Aria rose and crossed to the cradle first. She laid two fingers against the baby's chest until his breathing settled again. Izack watched her in silence, the notebook loose in his hand.

"You know," she said without turning, "formal names are only the outer structure."

"Yes."

"Children make the rest themselves."

She looked back at him over her shoulder. In the lantern-light she seemed both tired and indestructible.

"Alexander Rune Thorne will become whatever kind of man he chooses. If we are given Lyra Starveil, Mira Lightscribe, or Senna Fizzle, they will do the same. Names are blessings, not instructions."

Izack's face gentled.

"You always make the theory better."

"No," Aria said. "I make it usable."

He stood and came to her side. For a while neither of them spoke. They simply looked down at their son, at the improbable smallness of him, at the hand still half-open above the blanket as if he were already holding a future they could not see.

"And if there is another boy?" Izack asked at last, voice low.

Aria considered that.

"Then he will need a name with enough shadow in it to survive being born after this one."

Izack smiled slightly. "I may have a thought."

"Of course you do."

"Nightwhisper."

She turned to look at him.

"That is very dramatic."

"Respectably dramatic."

"Dangerously close to one of your graphs."

"Aria."

She rested her head briefly against his shoulder.

"Keep it," she said. "For later."

Outside, the World Tree shimmered silver through the glass. Somewhere above them Polly gave one quiet, smug caw and then, mercifully, did not comment.

Izack slipped an arm around Aria's waist. She reached for his ink-stained hand and held it there between them, warm and human and real while the tower hummed around them.

"Lyra Starveil," he murmured, as if testing a blessing.

"Mira Lightscribe," Aria replied.

He smiled into her hair.

"Senna Fizzle."

This time Aria laughed outright, then caught herself before she woke the baby.

"If we truly name a child that," she whispered, "you are explaining it to every ambassador personally."

"Gladly."

"And to my father."

"With diagrams."

"That," she said, "is exactly what I was afraid of."
