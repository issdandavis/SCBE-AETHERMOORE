EDITORIAL REVIEW: THE SIX TONGUES PROTOCOL
By Issac Davis
Reviewed for craft, consistency, momentum, and KDP readiness

Reviewer note: This is a genuinely excellent novel. The technical-systems metaphor for magic is the book's greatest innovation and its greatest risk -- when it works, it produces some of the most original fantasy prose I have read in years. When it overreaches, it produces paragraphs that read like architecture documentation wearing a narrative costume. The notes below are calibrated to that tension. The goal is to sharpen, not to sand down.

---

### Chapter 1: Protocol Handshake
**Time passage**: Strong. The chapter moves from 3:14 AM coffee to the transit to Aethermoor with real felt duration. The Thursday flashback gives us Marcus's life in one paragraph. The arrival lands physically -- you feel him hit stone.

**Character consistency**:
- Marcus: Excellent. The engineer-brain voice is established immediately and never breaks. "I intend to go home, I intend to find out what's happening, I intend to not die in whatever this is" is perfect Marcus.
- Polly: Arrives fully formed. The sarcasm-over-care layering is clear from her first line. Feather-tells introduced organically (storm-flatten = fear for him, smoothing = something softer).
- Minor characters: None yet. Good.

**Momentum**: Extremely strong opening. The pull is curiosity plus survival. A reader will turn the page.

**Prose quality**: The opening three paragraphs are the weakest part. The cold coffee metaphor is belabored -- "The ghost of a drink pretending it still had a reason to be in the mug" is one metaphor too many in a paragraph that already has "flat and cold" and "not even bad, just nothing." The apartment description at line 24-30 (Thursday flashback) is beautiful and specific. The re-description at line 34 ("an efficiency unit in the Sunset District with a mattress on the floor and a fridge containing exactly one expired yogurt and a bottle of Sriracha") repeats what we already know.

**The "trying too hard" check**: The white void transit section (lines 60-86) slightly overperforms. "Through the floor of what was real, as if reality were a membrane" is strong. "Colors. Symbols. Mathematical structures that looked almost like geodesic music. Frequencies woven into geometries woven into meanings" is stacking. Pick one image.

**Specific line-level notes**:
- CUT: "The ghost of a drink pretending it still had a reason to be in the mug." (line 20) -- the paragraph is stronger without it.
- CUT: The second apartment description at line 34, from "His apartment was twelve miles south" through "His manager had stopped asking why." This is a recap of what we already showed better through the Thursday scene.
- TRIM: "Colors. Symbols. Mathematical structures that looked almost like geodesic music. Frequencies woven into geometries woven into meanings." -- pick "Frequencies woven into geometries" and cut the rest.
- The double-dash (--) should be em-dash throughout for KDP consistency.

**Rating**: 8/10. Strong hook. Would keep reading.

### Interlude: Polly's Vigil
**Time passage**: Perfect. The whole piece is about time -- five hundred years of it, compressed into one night of watching Marcus sleep. The vigil structure gives it natural rhythm.

**Character consistency**: This is where Polly becomes a great character. The revelation of Izack ("He'd fallen asleep with chalk in his hair and equations on his hands") establishes the emotional core of the entire book. Her internal conflict about hope is the best writing in the first 50 pages.

**Momentum**: Lower than Chapter 1 by design. This is a breathing space. The Seventh Holding tease at the end pulls the reader forward.

**Prose quality**: Excellent. "Hope was the most dangerous state in her emotional taxonomy" is a perfect Polly sentence. The ending ("Marcus dreamed of coffee. The system breathed.") is restrained and exactly right.

**The "trying too hard" check**: Clean. This interlude earns every word.

**Specific line-level notes**: None. Do not touch this interlude.

**Rating**: 9/10.

### Chapter 2: The Language Barrier
**Time passage**: Good. We move from corridor to cathedral to refectory to sky-bridge, each location shift giving a sense of Marcus's hours passing.

**Character consistency**: Marcus's engineer-brain is firing perfectly -- "Like virtual memory pages mapped to different process spaces" is how this man actually thinks. Polly's "I don't know what that means, but your face did the right thing" is her best recurring joke.

**Momentum**: The six-tongue introduction is the book's biggest exposition risk. It works because Marcus's recognition keeps it active rather than passive. He is not being told things. He is pattern-matching in real time.

**Prose quality**: Generally strong. One significant problem: "Synesthesia, his rational brain supplied. Sensory cross-wiring. Symptom of extreme stress, sleep deprivation, or neurological damage." (line 380-381) -- this TELLS after the previous paragraph already SHOWED the cross-wired senses beautifully. Cut the tell.

**The "trying too hard" check**: The cathedral description (lines 430-434) runs long without Marcus reacting. Break it with a thought or physical response around line 433.

**Specific line-level notes**:
- CUT: "Synesthesia, his rational brain supplied. Sensory cross-wiring. Symptom of extreme stress, sleep deprivation, or neurological damage." (lines 380-381) -- the showing is already perfect.
- CUT: "Or, said the part of his brain that was rapidly losing the argument, symptom of being in a place where the fundamental encoding of reality uses different compression." (lines 382-383) -- same problem, telling after showing.
- The seventh pedestal at lines 598-600 is a beautiful plant. Well done.

**Rating**: 7/10. Necessary chapter, carries risk of info-dump. Mostly succeeds.

### Chapter 4: The Swarm Beneath

**The "trying too hard" check**: One spot: "Engineering offered two sins: pretending every problem was technical, and pretending technical consequences weren't moral." (line 1369) -- this is a thesis statement that the chapter has already demonstrated. Consider cutting it; the reader already knows.

**Specific line-level notes**:
- The trapped intents reading HELP / NOT MALICIOUS / LOCKED BELOW (lines 1335-1339) is among the best moments in the book. Do not touch.

### Interlude: Jorren Records
**Time passage**: N/A (reflective interlude). The personal-journal format gives it its own temporal quality.

### Chapter 7: Fleet Dynamics

**Specific line-level notes**:

### Chapter 11: The Void Seed
**Time passage**: Strong. The descent feels like it takes longer than it does. Tovak's flask on the stairs is a perfect breathing beat.

**Character consistency**: Tovak speaks more in this chapter than anywhere else in the book, and every word matters. The moonflower spirit exchange is the chapter's best scene -- Tovak showing kindness through UM tradition.

**Momentum**: The Void Seed itself is terrifying because it is correct. "Not out of malice. The Nexus wasn't evil. It was correct. In the way that a proof can be correct and still catastrophic." This is the book's philosophical center.

**The "trying too hard" check**: The whisper from the Void Seed (lines 4728-4731) is slightly overwritten. "One source of truth. One verification. One mind that sees everything." would be sufficient without "No committee. No consensus. No delay. No compromise. Just... correctness."

**Specific line-level notes**:
- "Dimensional offset" as both weakness and defense is the chapter's key insight. Well constructed.
- The refugee count (1,200 over 10,000 years) and Polly's funeral count (200+) are devastating when placed together.

**Momentum**: The seventeen copies are a terrifying tactical concept. The three observers (who do nothing but watch) are the most frightening element -- "contained things that watch are waiting" pays off here.

**Prose quality**: The battle writing is clear and readable despite its complexity. Each copy-type (authority, grievance, demonstrators, peer-chain bootstrappers, runners, observers) is distinct and serves a tactical purpose.

The "I think I love you" is honest and clumsy and right. However, the kiss description (lines 5874-5878) runs slightly long for a moment that should be fast and messy.

**Specific line-level notes**:
- The third observer breaking silence with "The dead backlog has names. Someone should read them." (line 5766) is haunting.
- "We didn't win. We graduated." (line 5770) -- excellent.

### Chapters 14-18 (Threshold Country through The Fizzlecress Incident)
**Time passage**: Chapter 14 is the book's first external adventure -- Marcus leaving the Spire. The time jump to "eleven months" at the start of Chapter 15 is well-handled. Chapters 15-18 cover the passage of years through domestic detail (the door chord, the dining hall, the marketplace, teaching). This is the book's "ordinary life" movement and it is essential. Without it, the stakes would be abstract.

**Character consistency**: Marcus's vocabulary evolution is handled well -- by Chapter 15, he uses Aethermoor terms naturally ("need-state," "tongue phase") alongside his Earth analogies. Polly's feather-tells remain consistent. Senna's cuff-touching escalates as intimacy deepens.

**Momentum**: Chapter 15 (The Ordinary) is deliberately slow. This is a risk. Some readers will want to skip ahead. But the domestic details pay dividends later -- Havelock's daughter, Yoon-Ji, the marketplace -- because they make Aethermoor a PLACE rather than a SYSTEM.

**Prose quality**: Chapter 16 (The Fractal Proof) is technically the densest chapter but works because the phi-invariant is explained through Marcus's discovery process rather than through exposition. The celebration scene is warm and earned. Chapter 18 (Fizzlecress) is the book's funniest chapter. Fizzle Brightcog is a joy. Dax's "you absolute drongo" speech is the emotional truth the book needed.

**The "trying too hard" check**: Chapter 16 has one significant overwrite -- the Council presentation section (around lines 7270-7340) recaps the proof in too much technical detail. The reader already understands phi; what matters is the REACTION. Trim the re-explanation by 30%.

**Specific line-level notes**:
- Chapter 15, the door chord scene: "Sing it like you mean it. The door doesn't care about your technique. It cares about your commitment." -- peak Bram.
- Chapter 17, the Void Seed's new argument: "A system confident in its own correctness does not need external validation." -- this is the strongest Void Seed exchange in the book. Keep.
- Chapter 18, the kimchi jjigae passage: This is where Marcus's Earth grief finally gets its full expression. Seven years of suppressed homesickness released through bloom liquor. Essential.

**Rating**: Ch14: 8/10. Ch15: 7/10. Ch16: 8/10. Ch17: 9/10. Ch18: 10/10.

### Chapter 25-26: The Underroot / The Time Tax
**Time passage**: The Underroot chapter is tunnel-time -- compressed, tense, lit by warrant-light. Dremsreach is cyclic time made physical. Both chapters give Marcus new temporal experiences that deepen the worldbuilding.

**Momentum**: Strong. The Voss encounter adds urgency. The Dremsreach probes confirm the Dominion threat. The Senna confession at the herepoint is the relationship's most honest moment.

**Prose quality**: The corridor-warrant system is one of the book's best inventions -- "a hall pass that was also an ongoing conversation with the floor." The Dremsreach phase-cycling is complex but the navigator metaphor (sailors feeling tide) makes it accessible.

**The "trying too hard" check**: The Underroot chapter is well-controlled. The Dremsreach chapter has one section where the attestation-disc explanation (three rings, what each means) could be compressed.

**Rating**: Ch25: 9/10. Ch26: 8/10.

## OVERALL ASSESSMENT

**Strengths**:
- The systems-as-magic conceit is original and brilliantly sustained across 142,000 words.
- Character voices are distinct. You can tell who is speaking without tags.
- The interludes are the book's secret weapon. They provide character depth that the main narrative can't.
- The Thorne family is the best fictional family I have encountered in recent fantasy. Each sibling's coping strategy (duty, speed, silence, continuity, correctness) maps to a real human response to family trauma.
- Marcus is a genuinely likeable protagonist whose growth feels earned.
- Polly is one of the great fantasy characters. Full stop.

**Weaknesses**:
- The opening three paragraphs are the weakest writing in the book. Fix them and the first impression improves dramatically.
- Show-then-tell is a recurring pattern. The writing SHOWS beautifully and then adds a TELL paragraph explaining what was just shown. Cut the tells.
- Metaphor density occasionally stacks 3+ metaphors in a paragraph. The strongest one is always enough.
- The middle chapters (15-16) risk losing momentum for readers who want plot over domesticity. This is a feature, not a bug, but be aware some readers will skim.
- Technical re-explanation in Council/presentation scenes. By the time Marcus presents to the Council, the reader already understands the concepts. Trust your audience.

**Izack reference consistency**: Consistent. The name Izack is spelled correctly throughout. His character is foreshadowed through portraits, Polly's grief, and Kael's backstory before he appears. His arrival in Chapter 29 is the payoff the entire book has been building toward.

**Seventh pedestal / seventh tongue foreshadowing**: Well planted. First appearance at the end of Chapter 2 (line 598-600). Referenced through Kael's research, the Bonecasters, Edda's bone tablet, and finally named by Izack in the observatory. The progression from "empty pedestal" to "seventh silence" to "witness as governance" is the book's deepest thematic thread and it pays off fully.

**Polly's feather-tells**: Consistent throughout. Storm-flatten = distress/alarm. Slow-smooth = comfort. Full-body shake = internal reset. Press-flat = concern. The consistency creates a reliable emotional language that the reader learns alongside Marcus.

**Marcus's vocabulary evolution**: Handled well. Early chapters: pure Earth tech analogies (TCP handshakes, virtual memory, authorization middleware). Middle chapters: mixed (using both Earth terms and Aethermoor vocabulary). Late chapters: Aethermoor terms dominant with occasional Earth callbacks for emphasis.

**KDP formatting issues found**:
- Double-dash (--) used throughout instead of em-dash. Must be converted.
- Ellipsis inconsistency (some ".." found, should all be "...").
- Chapter headers use markdown (#). Need plain text for KDP.
- Scene breaks use "* * *" which is correct for KDP.
- Some quotation mark inconsistency (straight vs. curly).

**Final verdict**: This is a publishable novel that needs craft-level polish, not structural revision. The story is complete. The characters are alive. The world is original. Fix the opening, cut the tells, thin the metaphor stacks, and this book will find its audience.
