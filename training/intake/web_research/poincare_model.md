# Poincaré Disk Model

The Poincaré disk model, also called the conformal disk model, represents 2-dimensional hyperbolic geometry within a unit disk. All points are inside the unit disk, and straight lines are either circular arcs contained within the disk that are orthogonal to the unit circle or diameters of the unit circle. The disk model was first described by Bernhard Riemann in 1854, later popularized by Henri Poincaré in 1882.

## Distance Formula

For two distinct points p and q inside the disk, the hyperbolic distance uses the cross-ratio of the points with the ideal boundary points. For vectors u and v with norm less than 1:

δ(u,v) = 2||u−v||² / ((1−||u||²)(1−||v||²))

The hyperbolic distance is then d(u,v) = arcosh(1 + δ(u,v)).

## Metric Tensor

The associated metric tensor is ds² = 4||dx||² / (1−||x||²)². This conformal metric preserves angles between intersecting curves. The Gaussian curvature of the hyperbolic disk is K = −1, establishing it as a constant negative curvature space.

## Construction of Hyperbolic Lines

The unique hyperbolic line through two points P and Q (not on a diameter) requires finding inversions in the boundary circle, computing midpoints, drawing perpendiculars, finding the circle center, and drawing the orthogonal arc. Lines are defined analytically by x² + y² + ax + by + 1 = 0.

## Cycles

There are four types of cycles in the Poincaré disk. Circles are Euclidean circles completely inside the disk with hyperbolic center differing from Euclidean center. Horocycles are circles tangent to the boundary whose perpendicular geodesics converge asymptotically. Hypercycles are arcs intersecting the boundary at non-right angles, equidistant from a given hyperbolic line. Geodesics are arcs orthogonal to the boundary or diameters.

## Relations to Other Models

The Klein disk model uses straight Euclidean chords but is non-conformal. Conversion between Poincaré (x,y) and Klein coordinates uses the factor 2/(1+x²+y²). The Poincaré half-plane model relates via Möbius transformation: z = i(1+u)/(1−u). The hyperboloid model projects to the Poincaré disk using perspective projection from point [-1, 0, ..., 0].

## Key Properties

The model is conformal (preserves angles locally), isometric (distances follow Cayley-Klein metric), has constant curvature K = −1, provides a compact representation where the entire hyperbolic plane fits in a finite disk, and allows intuitive visualization of hyperbolic concepts. M.C. Escher used this model in his Circle Limit series (1958-1960) to visualize infinity on a finite plane.
