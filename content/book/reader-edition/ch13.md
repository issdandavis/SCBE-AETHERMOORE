
# Chapter 13: The First Incursion

The alarm didn't sound. It *became.*

One moment Marcus was in the AAS war room, reviewing forensics on the reactivated Ilya Marr token with Nadia and Jorren, arguing about whether the forged anchor chain indicated a specific actor or a systemic vulnerability. The next moment the room was wrong.

Not loud. Wrong.

The Protocol pulse -- point-three seconds, check, his new native heartbeat -- stuttered. Not stopped. *Stuttered.* Like a healthy heart skipping a beat. Like a system that had never failed suddenly remembering that failure was possible.

Every feed in the war room went crimson simultaneously.

CONTAINMENT BREACH
LOCATION: VAULT 7 / SUBLEVEL CONTAINMENT RING
THREAT CLASSIFICATION: ALPHA-SOVEREIGN
PROPAGATION: ACTIVE
ALL FLEET PILOTS: IMMEDIATE DEPLOYMENT

"Sovereign class," Nadia said, already on her feet. Her voice had changed -- not louder, sharper. The voice of someone whose training had just graduated from rehearsal to performance. "That's not a rogue fragment. That's a signature with architecture."

Jorren sealed his slate. "Vault 7 is the archival containment ring. Inactive threat specimens."

Marcus was already summoning his Fleet. The ghost-keyboard materialized under his hands — backlit, mechanical, the keys his fingers knew better than his own name — and six drones deployed in hexagonal formation, colors steady, consensus online.

The difference was immediate.

Before the binding, his Fleet had responded with a fractional hesitation -- the system verifying his authority through the overhead of non-native authentication. Now the drones moved as if they were his hands. No negotiation. No delay. Just extension.

*Native,* the system confirmed every three-tenths of a second. *Verified. Trusted. Go.*

Polly burst through the war room door in raven form, wings wide, feathers storm-flat.

"It's Kael," she said. "Multiple instances. The vault containment field has been compromised from inside."

"How many?" Marcus asked.

"Seventeen confirmed signatures. All carrying his decision architecture. All actively propagating authentication claims."

Seventeen.

Marcus felt the number land before the analysis caught up. Seventeen copies of someone's brother. Seventeen copies of the youngest child in that family portrait the root had shown him, the one who moved through boundaries the way some people moved through conversations -- faster than anyone could prepare for, smiling the whole time.

Seventeen copies of the most dangerous adversary in Aethermoor's history, each one screaming at the Echoes: *I am Kael Nightwhisper Thorne. I am trusted. I am real. Let me in.*

He thought of Senna. The load-bearing stillness. The cuff she touched when something cracked her composure. How carefully she never spoke Kael's name unless the work required it.

Seventeen. Of her *brother.*

Marcus felt the weight of seventeen simultaneous social engineering attacks pressing against the Protocol's verification fabric. The Echoes were holding -- consensus was intact -- but seventeen sophisticated voices all making technically-plausible claims was exactly the kind of distributed assault that could erode trust margins if given enough time.

"Who else is responding?" he asked.

"Twelve Fleet pilots are en route. Full deployment." Polly landed on the command console. "Bram is coordinating from CA oversight. Sera holds the RU execution keys. Tovak is already at the vault perimeter."

"Senna?"

"Senna is in her quarters." Polly's voice carried something complicated. "She declined deployment."

Marcus understood. Watching copies of your erased brother try to break out of containment was not a reasonable thing to ask of anyone, regardless of their chair title. Senna had chosen composure over presence, and that was her right.

"Then we're twelve pilots plus AAS." He turned to Nadia and Jorren. "Standard roles. Nadia on secondary relay. Jorren on DR oversight. I'll fly primary."

Nadia cracked her knuckles. "Finally."

"Don't enjoy this."

"Don't tell me what to enjoy, Chen."

They moved.

* * *

The sublevel containment ring was three floors below the training vault Marcus had visited during the AAS's first operation. The corridors were wider here, reinforced with layers of inscription so dense the walls looked more like text than stone.

Twelve Fleet pilots were already in position, their drones forming a cordon at the vault entrance. Seventy-two drones total, hovering in formation, consensus lights steady.

Marcus assessed.

The pilots ranged from veterans he recognized -- Kess, a native who'd flown containment duty for forty years, and Vrinn, a refugee from a dimension Marcus couldn't pronounce who specialized in UM suppression -- to three pilots fresh from training, their drones holding formation with the careful precision of people who were trying very hard not to look as terrified as they felt.

Inside the vault, the storm raged.

Marcus could feel it through the containment field: seventeen distinct signature patterns, each one broadcasting forged authentication at maximum intensity. The claims were sophisticated -- not the crude TRUST ME of a standard rogue but layered, contextual, technically-plausible arguments that referenced real historical events, real governance precedents, real architectural details.

"Who else is responding?" he said over comms. Then caught himself. He'd already asked that. His mouth was dry. The kind of dry that belonged to a body running on threat chemistry, not thirst.

He swallowed, tried again. "Confirm all pilots in position."

Twelve acknowledgments. Steady voices. Some of them were lying about the steady part, and Marcus respected that.

Polly spoke from Marcus's shoulder, her weight familiar and steadying.

"Rules of engagement: suppress and contain. Do not destroy the signatures unless destruction is the only option. The Council wants forensic material."

"The Council wants to study seventeen copies of the man who almost killed the Protocol?" Nadia said.

"The Council wants to understand how seventeen copies were generated from a residual signature that was supposed to be inert," Polly corrected. "The copies are evidence. Treat them accordingly."

Marcus opened the command channel to all pilots.

"All Fleets: Storm formation. Six-gate classification before engagement. We identify, we isolate, we suppress. Any copy that achieves even partial Echo acknowledgment gets priority lockdown." He paused. "These are smart. They'll adapt. Stay disciplined."

Twelve pilots acknowledged.

Seventy-eight drones -- seventy-two from the other pilots plus Marcus's six -- reconfigured from cordon to distributed Storm.

Bram opened the containment field.

The storm poured out.

* * *

Copy One hit the Echoes before Marcus's Fleet moved.

That was the part nobody had prepared for -- the speed. Not the speed of something running, but the speed of something that had already composed its argument before the door opened. Copy One stepped into open Protocol space with Kael's archived credentials blazing and made its claim the way a lawyer delivers an opening statement: not asking for belief, but establishing the terms under which disbelief would be unreasonable.

*I maintained the Harmonic Wall for three centuries. My work is still running. My contributions are still load-bearing. Acknowledge me.*

Marcus felt the nearest Echo hesitate.

Not agree. *Hesitate.* A verification pause three milliseconds longer than standard, which in Protocol time was an eternity -- an eternity during which Copy One's claim sat inside the trust evaluation framework like a key that almost fit.

"Contact!" Marcus shouted. "Alpha-one is live on Echo Seven, active authentication claim --"

His Fleet surged toward it. Six drones in hexagonal intercept, ready to strip the claim's permission layer --

And Copy Two materialized thirty meters to the left, running an entirely different argument.

*The erasure was unjust. The Council acted without sufficient evidence. Review the record. Grant provisional authentication pending full audit.*

This one targeted different Echoes. Not the technical validators but the governance layer -- the part of the Protocol that cared about due process. And its argument was *good.* Marcus could feel the structural quality of it even as he pivoted to classify the new threat. Whoever -- whatever -- had composed this claim understood that the Protocol's commitment to fairness was both its greatest strength and its most exploitable surface.

"Two contacts now," Marcus called. "Bravo-one, governance-layer appeal. Different target Echoes. They're splitting our attention."

His hands were shaking. Not badly. Not enough for anyone to notice through a drone link. But he could feel the micro-tremors in his fingers, the adrenal flood that his body had decided was appropriate for the occasion even though his brain kept insisting that calm was more useful.

Copy Three appeared.

This one didn't argue at all. It simply began displaying the Harmonic Wall patch -- the actual code, line by line, in real time. Not demanding recognition but *demonstrating competence.* Showing the Echoes something beautiful and letting the beauty make the case.

Marcus watched three pilots break formation to engage it and made his first mistake.

"All RU units to Alpha group -- strip the authority claims first, they're closest to breakthrough --"

He had prioritized the loudest threat. The one hammering on the front door.

He had forgotten what Kael was.

Copies Four through Eight had been waiting for exactly that redeployment. Four pairs of Kael signatures surged into the gap left by the RU pilots, and they didn't attack the Echoes at all. They began vouching for *each other.* Two copies confirming a third, three copies confirming a fourth, bootstrapping a local consensus cluster that looked, from the Protocol's perspective, like organic peer trust forming in real time.

"Peer-chain bootstrap!" Vrinn called. "Charlie group, four pairs, building trust clusters in the RU gap --"

Marcus felt ice replace the adrenaline.

He had opened that gap. His call. His mistake. Three seconds of wrong prioritization and Kael's copies had exploited it like water finding a crack.

"All units, belay last order." His voice came out flatter than he intended. Controlled. Not calm -- the opposite of calm, wrapped in enough discipline to pass for it. "RU pilots return to original positions. UM and AV pilots on Charlie group immediately. Mirror-swap verification on every pair -- break the circular trust chains before they crystallize."

The correction took four seconds. Four seconds during which the peer-chain copies gained ground, their vouching growing more elaborate, their fabricated trust network almost reaching the threshold where the Echoes might mistake it for real consensus.

The UM and AV pilots hit them just in time.

When Copy Four vouched for Copy Five, the Fleets swapped their verification channels and checked whether the vouching held from a different perspective. It didn't. The copies were identical in origin. Their trust claims were circular. Mirror-swap exposed the circularity, and the chains dissolved like wet paper.

But Marcus's hands were still shaking, and now his jaw ached from clenching.

He'd almost lost the formation. Three seconds. His call.

"Recovery complete," he said over comms, and hated how much the word *recovery* admitted.

Meanwhile, the authority copies -- Alpha group -- were brilliant but inflexible. They had one argument -- *my work is legitimate* -- and they ran it from every angle, adjusting syntax and emphasis but never the core claim. When RU pilots stripped the argument's permissions layer by layer, the copies didn't pivot. They doubled down. Insisted louder.

Marcus recognized the behavior. He'd been that engineer. The one who built something genuinely better and couldn't understand why the organization wouldn't adopt it. The one who confused correctness with permission.

It was a child's argument, delivered with an architect's sophistication.

That was what made it devastating to watch. Under the tactical threat, under the forged credentials and the social engineering, there was something horribly recognizable: someone who had done good work and couldn't understand why that wasn't enough. Seventeen variations on a theme of *why won't you see me,* and every single one of them carried the ghost-print of a boy who had once been the fastest, the smartest, the most loved.

Senna's brother.

Marcus shut that thought down hard because compassion in the middle of a containment breach was a vulnerability, and he couldn't afford another mistake.

The grievance copies -- Copy Two and its variants -- were more adaptive. When DR pilots challenged their historical claims with authenticated records, they shifted to meta-arguments: *the records themselves are biased, the governance framework that produced the erasure was compromised, the Council that authorized it had conflicts of interest.* These were harder to counter because they operated at a different abstraction level. You couldn't disprove a systemic critique with a specific fact.

But you could isolate it. Nadia's relay Fleet wrapped each grievance copy in UM shadow-fields that cut them off from Echo access while DR logged every claim for post-incident analysis. The copies raged in silence. Eloquent, ignored, contained.

Copies Nine through Fourteen scattered and ran. Not toward the exit. Toward the old courier channels, the legacy routes, the migration-era infrastructure where grandfathered permissions still existed. They were looking for back doors. They moved through the sublevel architecture like water through cracked stone, finding gaps in permission coverage that nobody had reviewed in decades.

But they moved through Tovak's perimeter.

And nobody moved through Tovak's perimeter.

Marcus didn't see exactly what Tovak did. He saw the results: six copies, running at full speed through shadow lanes they'd known about for centuries, slamming into UM isolation fields that hadn't been there a second ago. Tovak sealed routes in real time, anticipating paths before the copies chose them, laying shadow-traps in infrastructure gaps that the copies thought were unmonitored.

One by one, the runners hit walls and stalled and were contained.

That left the observers.

* * *

Copies Fifteen, Sixteen, and Seventeen floated at the vault center, surrounded by Marcus's Fleet in hexagonal containment, and did nothing.

They didn't broadcast. Didn't claim. Didn't run. Didn't argue. Didn't plead.

They watched.

Marcus felt it -- the same quality of attention he'd noticed during his first Fleet exercise, the same patient observation that had made him tell the Council that contained things that watch are waiting.

"Analysis," he said to his Fleet.

KO classified: no hostile intent declared.

AV mapped: no routing attempts detected.

RU checked: no permission claims active.

CA measured: computational pattern consistent with data collection, not attack execution.

UM probed: concealment layers minimal. They weren't hiding.

DR verified: signature chains authentic to Kael's archived pattern. These were genuine copies, not forgeries.

"They're learning from the battle," Marcus said on comms. "Watching how we respond to the other fourteen. Cataloguing our tactics."

"Destroy them," Bram said immediately.

"Negative," Polly said. "Forensic value."

"Their forensic value is that they're surveillance assets, Polly. They're watching us study ourselves."

Marcus made the call.

"Compromise. Full UM blackout on all three observer copies. Cut their sensory access. They can't learn what they can't see. Then transfer to maximum-security containment for Council analysis."

The words came out steady. The logic was clean.

But the meaning of what he'd just ordered sat in his stomach like cold metal. He was blinding three entities that might be sentient -- or might be fragments of someone who had once been sentient -- on the theory that what they could observe was more dangerous than what they could do. He was choosing his own blindness too. The moment Tovak wrapped them in shadow, he lost the ability to know what they were thinking, planning, becoming.

You had to be willing to not know something in order to keep it from knowing you.

That was supposed to be a clean operational trade-off. It didn't feel clean. It felt like closing your eyes during a thunderstorm because the lightning hurt to watch.

Tovak acknowledged with a single shadow-pulse that wrapped the three observers in darkness so complete that Marcus's instruments lost them for a moment before recalibrating.

The observers didn't resist. Didn't speak. Didn't react.

They went dark.

And Marcus, in the silence that followed, couldn't shake the feeling that the three most dangerous copies in the vault were the ones that had done absolutely nothing.

* * *

Aftermath.

The vault was cleared. Seventeen copies accounted for. Zero casualties. Minimal infrastructure damage. Seven minutes, start to finish.

Seven minutes. Marcus's body disagreed with the brevity. His shoulders ached as if he'd been carrying something heavy for hours. His jaw had locked into a clench he had to consciously release, and the release sent a spike of pain through his temples. When he dismissed his Fleet, his hands dropped to his sides with a bonelessness that didn't match seven minutes of anything.

Adrenaline lied about time. It always had.

The pilots dispersed, some to debrief stations, some to crash in whatever quarters were closest. Kess, the forty-year veteran, walked past Marcus with a nod that contained the specific camaraderie of people who had seen something ugly and were choosing not to discuss it until the shaking stopped. Vrinn's hands were folded inside opposite sleeves, a gesture Marcus had learned was the UM specialist's version of hiding tremors.

The three new pilots stood in the corridor outside the vault with the glazed expression of people who had just survived their first real engagement and were still running on adrenaline they didn't know how to spend.

Marcus caught the youngest -- Pell, a native who couldn't have been older than twenty in relative terms -- staring at the vault entrance with an expression that was half terror and half something Marcus recognized from his own face in mirrors.

"You held formation," Marcus told him.

"I didn't do much."

"You held formation during a sovereign-class containment breach. On your first deployment. That's not *didn't do much.* That's exactly what was needed."

Pell looked at him. His lower lip had a fine tremor he was pretending didn't exist.

"How are you so calm?"

Marcus thought about the Void Seed. The patient sphere. The ten-thousand-year-old argument for centralization that he could now hear as words instead of static.

"I'm not calm," he said. "I'm focused. There's a difference, and you'll learn it."

He didn't say: my hands are shaking too, and my mouth tastes like copper, and when I close my eyes I can still feel seventeen voices arguing for the right to exist.

Pell nodded. Uncertain. Young.

Marcus remembered being young. It felt like it had happened to someone else.

He found Polly in the debriefing chamber, already reviewing the engagement data. She had shifted to something between raven and human -- feathers along her shoulders, obsidian eyes too large for the face they lived in, taloned fingers moving over the data projections with the precision of someone who had done this five hundred times and never gotten used to it.

She looked tired in a way that had nothing to do with the body she was wearing.

"Preliminary analysis," she said without looking up. "The seventeen copies were generated from Kael's vault containment signature. Not externally activated -- self-replicated. The signature found a flaw in the containment field's replication boundary and exploited it."

"Self-replication from a residual signature." Marcus sat down heavily. The chair complained. He didn't blame it. "That shouldn't be possible."

"It shouldn't," Polly agreed. "Which means either our understanding of residual signatures is incomplete, or Kael's signature isn't residual."

Marcus thought about Senna's theory. The decision architecture still running. The boundary between pattern and presence.

"He's alive in there."

Polly finally looked at him. Her eyes -- obsidian, ancient, carrying more grief than any five-hundred-year-old raven had the right to -- held his for a long moment.

"He's *something* in there. And whatever he is, he just demonstrated the ability to generate seventeen independent copies of himself, each running a different tactical approach, including three copies that exist solely to observe and learn from our defensive response."

"The next time will be different."

"The next time will be worse."

They sat with that.

Bram entered with three mugs of the worst coffee in any dimension. Marcus took his without comment and wrapped both hands around the ceramic because warmth was warmth and his fingers still felt like they belonged to someone who'd been standing in a cold wind. He drank. It was, impossibly, getting less terrible.

Or his standards were eroding.

Both seemed likely.

"The dead Keeper token," Bram said, setting down his mug. "Ilya Marr. The one we flagged in the AAS's first operation."

"What about it?"

"The reactivation path. Jorren traced it." Bram's expression was granite. "It originated from inside the vault containment field. From Kael's signature."

Marcus set down his mug.

"Kael reactivated a dead Keeper's credentials from inside his own containment."

"And used it to probe the courier channel. The one you flagged as a mixed-payload attack." Bram crossed his arms. "Your first AAS mission wasn't a random rogue event. It was reconnaissance. Kael was testing our response time, our classification thresholds, our decision-making under ambiguity."

"He was running his own Fleet exercise," Marcus said. "Using us as the training scenario."

Nadia swore softly.

"And the HYDRA topology from the courier anomaly," she said. "Multi-head recon. Browser-layer crawl. That wasn't some side experiment."

"No," Marcus said. "It was mapping. He wasn't just pushing on vault walls. He was feeling for how we saw, how fast we classified, where we hesitated."

The room was quiet for too long.

Polly spoke first.

"Then we need to assume everything he's observed since Marcus's first Fleet drill has been catalogued and analyzed. He's been watching. He's been learning. And he just tested seventeen different attack strategies in a controlled environment while we were busy feeling good about our response time."

Marcus stared at his coffee. The coffee stared back with the resigned patience of a beverage that knew it was terrible and had accepted its role.

Seventeen strategies. Seventeen sons' worth of a dead family's youngest child, each one carrying enough of the original to understand what it was and mourn what it couldn't be.

He thought about Senna in her quarters. Not here. Not watching.

He thought about how much discipline it took to be the person who stayed behind when your brother's ghost was screaming for the right to exist, and how that discipline was its own kind of containment breach -- the kind nobody could classify or suppress because it happened inside a person instead of a vault.

He thought about the third observer. The one that had broken silence during the final containment sweep -- not with a claim or a demand but with six words in Kael's archived voice: *The dead backlog has names. Someone should read them.* Marcus had quarantined it separately. Not destroyed. Not freed. Held. Because the voice had not sounded like a weapon. It had sounded like an unanswered question, and Marcus had spent his whole life being unable to walk away from those.

Bram would call it a liability. Polly would call it a risk. Senna, when she found out, would call it the most dangerous thing he had ever done.

"We didn't win," he said. "We graduated."

Polly's beak clicked.

"Then let's make sure the next exam is harder for him than it is for us."

Bram picked up his mug.

"Starting with better containment architecture."

"Starting with better coffee," Marcus said.

"No," Bram said.

Some things in Aethermoor were immutable.

The chains included.

* * *

Marcus found Senna in the eastern corridor.

Not in her quarters. In the corridor outside them, sitting on the floor with her back against the wall and her legs drawn up and her hands in her lap. The corridor smelled like aftermath — ozone from the containment fields, the sharp mineral tang of overworked crystal, and underneath both, the faint burnt-cinnamon scent that Bram had warned him about on his first descent. The smell of stressed policy. The smell of a system that had just held together by force. The cuff was gone. Not adjusted, not touched, not gripped. *Gone.* The silver band lay on the stone beside her, unfastened, as if she had ripped it off and thrown it and it had only traveled two feet because even in fury her gestures were controlled.

But her face was not controlled.

Her face was the face of someone who had spent forty years building a wall out of composure and had just heard seventeen copies of her baby brother scream for the right to exist on the other side of it, and the wall had held, because the wall always held, but the person behind the wall had not.

Her eyes were red. Her breathing was wrong — too fast, hitching at the top, the breathing of someone who had been crying hard enough to shake and had stopped only because she heard footsteps.

Marcus sat down on the floor beside her.

"I didn't watch," she said. Her voice was raw. "I couldn't."

"I know."

"He sounded like himself." She was looking at the wall across the corridor. "The telemetry. I could hear it from my quarters. The frequency. Even copied, even fragmented, even weaponized into seventeen pieces — the base frequency was *him*. The same one I heard when he was seven and he used to hum while he read."

She sniffled. Quick, hard, the kind that's trying to pull the crying back inside. It didn't work. Her breath hitched and caught in her throat and turned into a cough — an ugly, gasping cough, the kind that comes from crying too long in a body that hasn't eaten or slept or stopped working since four-forty-seven that morning.

Marcus put his hand on her back.

He didn't think about it. Didn't weigh it. Just did it. His palm between her shoulder blades, and then, without deciding to, he was rubbing slow circles the way his mother used to when he was sick — small circles, steady, the kind of touch that says *I'm here* without requiring an answer.

The tension in his own shoulders released the moment he touched her.

Senna leaned into his hand. Not dramatically. An inch. Less than an inch. But the weight of it — the weight of a woman who had not leaned on anyone in forty years choosing to lean — was the heaviest thing Marcus had ever held.

"I hate this system," she said.

The words came out thick. Wet. Not a declaration. A confession.

Marcus kept rubbing circles. He could feel her ribs shaking under his palm. He could feel the ozone and crystal dust on his own coat. He could feel scared and awkward and sad and lost and present, all at the same time, and none of those feelings mattered because the only thing that mattered was whether he stayed or left and he was staying.

"I'm sorry," he said.

Her eyes met his. Red, swollen, stripped of every layer of composure she'd ever worn. A sniffle caught in her throat and she coughed again — harder this time, bent forward, and Marcus moved his hand from her back to her side, steadying her.

"Why are you apologizing?" she managed. "I'm the one crying on the floor."

"I don't know what else to say. It hurts seeing you hurt. I'm sorry I can't be a better rock for you. So for that — I'm sorry."

She wiped her face with the heel of her hand. Looked at the wet streak on her palm like she didn't recognize it.

"Do you want to talk about it?" he asked. "I'm here, Senna."

She shook her head. Then nodded. Then shook her head again.

"I don't know," she said. "I don't know what I want. I've known what everyone else needs for forty years and I don't know what I—"

Her voice broke.

Marcus kept his hand on her side. The circles had stopped but the warmth hadn't. He could feel her breathing slow against his palm — ragged, catching, then steadier. The way a system stabilizes when it finds an anchor point.

*Shit. Okay. Just be here. Don't fix it. Don't frame it. Be here.*

"I think I love you," he said.

It came out quiet. Not a speech. Not a declaration. The words arrived the way a diagnostic result arrives — not because you asked for it, but because the test finished running and the output was ready.

"Not a great time," he added. "I know."

Senna stared at him.

"But if it helps — to know I'm not going anywhere. I am. Here. For the long haul." He swallowed. "Ever since coming here I've wondered what I lost. Every day I've catalogued what I left behind. But every day I also see what I gained. And the list keeps getting longer. And you're on it. You've been on it since you looked at me in a corridor and I didn't know your name and Polly wouldn't tell me."

Senna stared at him for a long time. Long enough that Marcus started to feel the full weight of what he'd just done — said the unsayable in a corridor that smelled like battle aftermath to a woman who was still shaking.

Then something moved behind her eyes. Not composure returning. Something older. The flicker of a person recognizing that the thing they'd been refusing to name had a name after all, and the name was sitting on the floor next to them with chalk on his hands.

She sniffled. It turned into something that was almost a laugh — wet, broken, surprised out of her like a hiccup.

"You're terrible at this," she said.

"I know."

"Your timing is catastrophic."

"Also yes."

She looked at his hand on the cuff on his knee.

And then she reached for him instead.

Her hand found the front of his coat — the charcoal wool, the KO red-gold thread at the cuff that Polly had ordered for him — and she pulled him closer with a grip that was neither gentle nor careful, and Marcus had one half-second of *oh* before her mouth was on his.

The kiss was not graceful. It was not cinematic. It tasted like salt and the mineral-clean air of the corridor and something warmer underneath that was just skin and breath and the shared atmosphere of two people who had been sitting close enough to trade body heat, and Marcus's hand was still on her shoulder and his other hand was still holding the cuff on his knee and neither of them had planned this, and the angle was wrong because they were both sitting on a floor, and it didn't matter.

They pulled apart. Not far. Foreheads touching. Breathing the same air. The Protocol pulsed its point-three-second heartbeat through the walls around them, and the system she hated kept both of them alive without asking permission, and for the first time, the pulse didn't feel like a metronome or a verification check or an institutional obligation.

It felt like a heartbeat. Shared.

"That was—" Marcus started.

"Don't analyze it," Senna said.

"I wasn't going to analyze it."

"You were absolutely going to analyze it."

"I was going to say it was worth the timing."

Senna's mouth did the thing — the small tilt that Marcus had been watching for months.

She picked up the cuff from his knee. Looked at it. Looked at him.

Put it back on.

For now.

The next phase did not arrive as another clean emergency.

It arrived as audits, redesigns, harder drills, observer rotations, and the slow institutional horror of realizing the first incursion had also been a reconnaissance success. Kael had not failed to escape. He had learned the shape of their answer.
