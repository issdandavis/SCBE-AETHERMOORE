
# Interlude: Polly's Vigil

---

He fell asleep on the archive floor.

Not gracefully. Not the way a mage settles into meditative rest or a traveler finds their bedroll in the dark. Marcus Chen fell asleep the way engineers fall asleep: mid-thought, one hand still reaching for a mug that wasn't there, his body finally overruling a brain that had been running on stale coffee and stubbornness for the better part of two days.

Polly sat on the shelf above him, raven-shaped again because she was comfortable and because nobody was watching, and studied the man the Protocol had delivered.

His breathing had settled into the deep, steady rhythm of genuine unconsciousness. No dreaming yet -- that would come later, when the Protocol began its integration work and his sleeping mind tried to process frequencies it had no training for. She'd seen it before. The dreams were never pleasant.

She preened a wing feather. Then another. Methodical. Necessary. The kind of small maintenance that kept her from thinking about the last time she'd sat vigil over someone sleeping on this floor.

*Izack had slept here too. That first night, five hundred and twelve years ago, when the convergence point was still raw and the dimensional membrane was thin enough that the crystal walls hummed with the strain of holding reality together. He'd fallen asleep with chalk in his hair and equations on his hands and a smile she hadn't earned yet.*

She shook the memory away. Feathers resettled.

Marcus Chen. Thirty-two. Systems engineer. Kershaw-Lin corporate security. Three monitors, dead coffee, an apartment he didn't live in. She'd read his Protocol intake assessment while he was still falling through the dimensional membrane, the system presenting his essential characteristics with the clinical precision of a personnel file authored by God.

**Coherence index: 0.92.**

That number had made her sit up on her perch and actually pay attention for the first time in decades.

Ninety-two percent coherence on first contact. Earth-native. No magical training, no attunement exercises, no decades of meditative alignment. Just a man whose declared intent matched his autonomous state with a precision that most Keepers spent their entire careers failing to achieve.

The Protocol's intake assessment had included a behavioral note she'd read three times:

*Subject demonstrates consistent pattern: when confronted with data that contradicts expectation, subject adjusts expectation rather than rejecting data. Adaptation rate: high. Defensive rigidity: low. Threat response: diagnostic rather than combative.*

A security engineer who, when faced with something he couldn't explain, tried to understand it instead of destroying it.

She'd waited five hundred years for that particular combination of traits.

Well. Five hundred and twelve, if she was being precise, which she always was, because precision was the difference between history and nostalgia, and Polly had seen too many good minds dissolve into the latter to tolerate it in herself.

* * *

The crystal archive breathed around them. Not literally -- the books hummed, the shelves resonated, the carved symbols on the walls pulsed with the Protocol's 0.3-second heartbeat -- but the cumulative effect was respiratory. Inhale: the signatures strengthened, the verification chains tightened, the system confirmed itself. Exhale: a brief loosening, a moment of renewal, the architectural equivalent of blinking.

Marcus didn't notice. Of course he didn't. He was asleep.

But Polly noticed, the way she always noticed, the way she'd been noticing for half a millennium -- watching the breath of the system she'd helped build, checking for the stutters and catches that meant something was wrong, the way a parent listens for changes in a sleeping child's breathing without consciously deciding to listen.

The system was tired.

Not failing. Not degraded. Just... carrying weight. The kind of accumulated structural fatigue that came from running at capacity for too long without the maintenance it needed. Kael's ghost-patches were holding, as they always did, because whatever else could be said about the boy, his engineering was extraordinary. But patches were not solutions. Patches were the architectural equivalent of "we'll fix it properly later," and later had been accumulating for forty years.

Polly hopped to the edge of the shelf and looked down at Marcus.

He'd curled onto his side in his sleep, one arm tucked under his head, the other extended toward where his desk would have been if he'd still been in San Francisco. His fingers twitched occasionally -- muscle memory reaching for a keyboard that wasn't there. The Protocol's verification light played across his face in slow pulses, painting him in alternating warmth and shadow.

He'd asked about coffee.

That was the thing. That was the thing that had cracked something in her chest that she'd spent decades convincing herself was sealed shut. Not the coherence score. Not the diagnostic instincts. Not the way he'd looked at the impossible and reached for explanations instead of weapons.

He'd asked about coffee.

*"Is it... a weapon?"* she'd asked, and she hadn't been performing ignorance. She'd genuinely wanted to hear how he'd explain the thing he was missing most. What people chose to mourn told you everything about what they loved, and Marcus Chen, dropped through the floor of reality into a world that shouldn't exist, running a seventy-two-hour clock on his continued existence, had mourned a mediocre cup of coffee.

Not power. Not status. Not his apartment or his career or his reflection in a mirror. A warm drink and the ritual of holding it.

*Izack had mourned chalk. The first week in the convergence space, before Avalon had walls or a name, he'd patted his pockets seventeen times looking for chalk that wasn't there, because chalk meant he could write equations, and equations meant the world was still something you could think about clearly.*

*They were the same, in the ways that mattered. Engineers who loved their tools because the tools meant they could keep working. People whose grief was practical rather than philosophical.*

*And Polly had loved them both on sight, which was a problem she'd been managing for five hundred years and apparently hadn't solved.*

She ruffled her feathers -- not the storm-flatten of distress or the slow smoothing of comfort, but the full-body shake that meant she was resettling something internal. Resetting. Deciding.

The last Architect had bound himself into the system's substrate to hold a gap closed that the six tongues couldn't govern. He was still down there, in the crystal observatory, smoking Fizzlecress and holding twenty-one dimensions together through sheer bloody-minded refusal to stop caring about calibration. She brought him tea. He didn't look up.

That was fine. That was architecture. Architecture was sacrifice shaped like a building, and Izack had always been better at building than at staying.

But Marcus wasn't Izack.

Marcus was the thing Izack had built a back door for: someone who could see the system from outside it, read its failures without being compromised by its assumptions, and care enough about coherence to ask the questions the system couldn't ask itself.

*What do the dead need from the living?*

*What happens to the things the system drops?*

*Where do endings go when there's no governance path for ending?*

Marcus had been in Aethermoor for less than six hours, and he'd already asked the question that five hundred years of Keepers had managed not to formulate. Not because they were stupid or negligent, but because the system they served had no vocabulary for it. You can't ask about the thing your language has no word for.

Unless you speak a different language entirely.

Unless you come from a world where systems crash and the error logs tell you why, where processes terminate and the operating system keeps running, where death is not an architectural impossibility but a Tuesday.

* * *

Below them, deep in the substrate, the Seventh Holding shifted.

Polly felt it the way she always felt it -- not as sound or vibration but as a change in the quality of the archive's silence. A slight densening. A gathering. As if the ghosts in the holding had sensed Marcus's presence and were paying attention the way the rest of the system was paying attention.

*Not yet,* she thought at them. *Give him time. He doesn't know what he is yet.*

The holding subsided. Patient. It had been patient for a very long time.

Marcus's fingers twitched again, reaching for a keyboard or a coffee mug or the last stable thing he remembered, and Polly felt something shift in her own architecture -- not magical, not systemic, but personal. The thing she'd sealed shut after Izack descended. The thing she'd maintained as a controlled absence in her own governance, the way you leave a shelf empty for a book that hasn't been written yet.

Hope.

She hated it. Hope was the most dangerous state in her emotional taxonomy, worse than grief, worse than rage, because hope made you build load-bearing structures on foundations that might not hold. Hope made you invest in futures that could collapse. Hope made you care about someone who had seventy-two hours to establish a permanent baseline and might not make it.

But she'd been doing this for five hundred years, and the Protocol hadn't sent anyone else, and Marcus Chen had asked about coffee, and Polly's relationship with hope was complicated in exactly the ways that mattered.

She hopped down from the shelf. Landed on the floor with the soft click of talons on crystal. Walked over to where Marcus was sleeping and settled herself approximately six inches from his outstretched hand, close enough to be present, far enough to maintain the professional distance that the situation required.

Tucked her head under her wing.

Made the small, involuntary sound that she would deny under oath was a sleep-caw but that anyone who'd spent time around ravens would recognize immediately: the soft, raspy vocalization of a bird who has decided that this particular spot, next to this particular person, is safe enough to close her eyes.

The archive hummed around them.

The Protocol pulsed its steady heartbeat.

And somewhere in the deep substrate, a tired man in a crystal chair felt something he hadn't felt in decades -- a change in the system's emotional weather, as subtle as the difference between a room where someone is sleeping alone and a room where someone is sleeping next to someone who chose to stay.

Izack didn't look up from his work.

But his pipe smoke curled a fraction more gently, and the seven corrupted facets of the observatory wall flickered, just for a moment, toward a color that might have been called hope if anyone had been there to name it.

Nobody was.

Polly slept.

Marcus dreamed of coffee.

The system breathed.
