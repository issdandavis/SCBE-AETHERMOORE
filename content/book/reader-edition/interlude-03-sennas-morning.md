
# Interlude: Senna Before Dawn

---

Senna Thorne woke at 4:47 AM because the continuity logs updated at 4:47 AM and she had been waking at 4:47 AM for eleven years and her body no longer required an alarm.

The room was dark. She let it be dark. Darkness was honest in a way that light sometimes wasn't -- it didn't ask you to perform wakefulness or competence or the particular flavor of composed authority that the Chair of Operational Continuity was expected to maintain during business hours. In the dark, Senna could be tired without consequence.

She allowed herself thirty seconds.

Thirty seconds of lying in bed with her eyes closed, feeling the weight of the quilts, the coolness of the pillow, the distant hum of the Protocol's heartbeat pulsing through the Spire's crystal infrastructure at the rate it had pulsed since before she was born. Point-three seconds. The breath of the system. She'd learned to feel it as a child, the way coastal children learn the tides -- not through study but through proximity, through years of sleeping in a building that breathed.

Her left hand found her right cuff. Touched the edge of the fabric. Adjusted it. A gesture so habitual it had ceased being conscious years ago, surviving only as the body's insistence on performing one small act of order before the day's larger orders demanded her attention.

Twenty-seven seconds left. Twenty-six. Twenty-five.

She thought about the Earth engineer sleeping somewhere in the guest quarters.

Marcus Chen. Coherence score: ninety-two percent. Diagnostic instincts that bordered on the architectural. A tendency to reach for systems metaphors that were so close to the truth that they circled back around from analogy to description. And eyes that sharpened when he was thinking and softened when he was feeling, which he did more often than he probably realized, and which she'd noticed because noticing things was her function and her burden and occasionally her consolation.

Fifteen seconds.

She thought about her brother.

Not the version of Kael that the Council discussed in closed session -- the threat vector, the exile, the cautionary tale about brilliance unchecked by collaboration. The other one. The one who sat beside her at the dinner table and forgot to eat because his ideas were bigger than his appetite. The one whose elbow touched hers when they both reached for the bread. The one who had been right about the gap, so completely and devastatingly right that the entire system was still carrying the consequences of ignoring him.

Five seconds.

She opened her eyes.

* * *

The continuity logs were waiting on her desk, which was waiting in her office, which was waiting in the administrative wing of the Spiral Spire, which was waiting for her the way the entire system was waiting for her -- patiently, expectantly, with the accumulated weight of a governance architecture that required constant verification and had chosen her as one of its verification points.

Senna dressed in the dark because she knew where everything was. Practical clothes -- the sort of garments that prioritized function over impression, that could withstand a sixteen-hour shift and still look deliberate at the end of it. Her hair went up in the arrangement that Lyra called "aggressively competent" and that Senna called "stays out of my face while I work."

Her cuff touched her wrist. She adjusted it.

The corridors of the Spire were quiet at this hour. Not empty -- the system never slept, and neither did the maintenance crew that kept its physical infrastructure functional -- but quiet in the way that institutions are quiet before dawn, when the space between the last shift and the first feels like a held breath.

She passed Bram's workshop on the way to her office. Light under the door. The smell of hot metal and strong tea, which meant Bram was either fixing something critical or had never gone to bed, or both. She considered knocking. Decided against it. Bram's version of care was expressed through infrastructure, not conversation, and interrupting him mid-repair was the social equivalent of interrupting a surgeon mid-incision.

She passed the guest quarters. Paused.

The door was closed. No light underneath. Marcus was sleeping, or doing a convincing impression of sleeping, which -- given his coherence scores -- was probably the former. The Protocol's verification light pulsed softly through the door frame, the pale blue-white of active monitoring, confirming that the guest pass was still valid and the subject was still extant.

Seventy-two hours. He had roughly fifty-four remaining.

Senna touched her cuff. Adjusted it. Walked on.

* * *

The continuity logs told her what she already knew and several things she'd hoped weren't true.

She read them the way she read everything -- systematically, completely, with the sort of attention that caught the gaps between the data points as clearly as it caught the data points themselves. Alexander called it her "negative space" reading -- the ability to see what the logs *weren't* saying with the same precision that other analysts applied to what they *were* saying.

Today, the negative space was loud.

Three more dead verification chains had been silently absorbed overnight. Two in the outer districts, where Keeper density was lower and the system's self-correction had more room to operate without anyone noticing. One in the mid-ring, close enough to core infrastructure that the redistribution had caused a measurable increase in processing load on adjacent nodes.

No log entries. No transition protocols. No acknowledgment.

The dead backlog was growing.

Senna pulled up the running total she maintained privately -- not in the official continuity reports, because the official reports didn't have a category for "deaths the system failed to acknowledge," and creating such a category would require Council approval, and Council approval would require explaining why the Chair of Operational Continuity had been tracking a governance gap that didn't officially exist.

Forty-seven. In the last calendar year alone. Forty-seven Keepers whose verification chains had gone dark and been silently absorbed without ceremony, without record, without the system so much as pausing to note that a person had stopped existing and the architecture was different because of their absence.

Kael had counted thirty-one in his first year of research. He'd been fourteen. She'd been eighteen. He'd shown her the numbers at the dinner table, and she'd felt something cold settle in her chest -- not surprise, because Senna had known the gap existed the way you know a crack in a foundation exists, by the patterns of stress in the walls above it. But confirmation was different from suspicion. Confirmation had weight.

"Fix the system," Kael had said, at fourteen.

"Replace the architecture," Kael had said, at sixteen.

The distance between those two sentences was eighteen months and the work of handlers who understood that the fastest way to weaponize a brilliant mind was to validate its frustrations until validation became dependency and dependency became direction.

Senna closed the private log. Opened the official continuity report. Began her morning review.

* * *

At 5:30 AM, Polly arrived.

The raven landed on the window ledge with the sort of precision that came from centuries of practice, her talons clicking on the crystal sill in a pattern that Senna recognized as "I am here and I have tea and you will drink it because I have decided you will drink it."

This was their ritual. Polly brought tea. Senna accepted tea. They sat in the pre-dawn office and said very little, because the things that mattered between them -- the shared knowledge of what the system was carrying, the shared grief for the people the system had dropped, the shared determination to keep the architecture functional until someone figured out how to make it better -- didn't require conversation. They required proximity. Witness. The physical fact of another consciousness choosing to be present in the same room at the same difficult hour.

"The Earth engineer had Protocol dreams," Polly said, settling onto her usual perch on the bookshelf above Senna's desk. "Standard integration type. The system is building his baseline."

"Will it hold?"

"His coherence is the highest I've seen from an Earth-native. Higher than some people who've been here for decades." A pause. Feather-smoothing -- the gentle kind, the kind that meant Polly was choosing her next words carefully. "He reminds me of someone."

Senna didn't ask who. She knew who. Everyone who spent time with Marcus Chen noticed the echoes -- the curiosity, the diagnostic instinct, the inability to stop working on a problem once he'd identified it. The differences were equally obvious: Marcus was warmer, less isolated, more willing to admit when he didn't understand something. But the core architecture was the same.

"Kael saw the gap at fourteen," Senna said.

"Yes."

"Marcus found the dead backlog in his first week."

"Yes."

"Does the system recruit people who can see what it can't see?"

Polly was quiet for long enough that Senna looked up from her logs to check whether the raven was still awake. She was. Her obsidian eyes were fixed on the middle distance, focused on something that existed in memory rather than in the room.

"Izack built a back door," Polly said finally. "Forty years ago. An edge-case recruitment pathway, designed to fire if the system was under sufficient distress and someone with the right cognitive architecture happened to be asking the right questions. He expected it to fire roughly never."

"It fired."

"It fired."

Senna touched her cuff. Adjusted it. Let the information settle.

"Will Marcus be enough?"

"I don't know." Polly's feathers ruffled -- not distress, not comfort, something between. "But he's what we have. And what he can see..." She trailed off. Then: "Kael's diagnosis was right, Senna."

"I know."

"The system needs to learn to govern its own endings."

"I know."

"Marcus can build it. Kael couldn't, because by the time he had the technical understanding, the handlers had redirected his methodology. But Marcus can build it because he's not trying to replace the system. He's trying to teach it to be better at what it already does."

"Fixing versus replacing."

"Exactly."

The tea was cooling in Senna's hands. She drank it anyway. It tasted the way Polly's tea always tasted -- warm, slightly herbal, adjusted to what your body needed rather than what your tongue expected. Today it tasted like resolve.

* * *

At 6:15 AM, the Spire began to wake.

Senna heard it the way she always heard it -- not as individual sounds but as a change in the system's ambient frequency. The maintenance crew's shift change. The first students arriving at the teaching levels. The Council members' offices activating their morning verification sequences. Bram's workshop door finally opening, followed by his heavy footsteps heading toward the commissary with the focused determination of a man who had been awake all night and required immediate sustenance.

She finished her continuity review. Filed the official report. Closed the private log. Straightened her desk -- not because it was messy, but because the act of ordering physical space was her way of ordering mental space, the external gesture that told her internal architecture *you are prepared for what comes next*.

What came next was another day of holding the system together.

Another day of tracking the gaps the system couldn't see.

Another day of carrying the weight that her brother had tried to carry alone, before the handlers turned his engineering into conquest and the boy who wanted to fix the world became the man who tried to replace it.

Senna stood. Touched her cuff. Adjusted it.

Walked out of her office and into the governed morning, where the Protocol's heartbeat pulsed its patient verification and the dead backlog grew by numbers that no one was counting except a woman who woke at 4:47 AM because someone had to.

Somewhere in the guest quarters, Marcus Chen was waking up.

Somewhere in the deep substrate, her father was holding the system together.

Somewhere -- she didn't know where, and the not-knowing was its own kind of weight -- her brother was alive or not alive, present or not present, and the system that had erased his name still ran his code in its load-bearing walls.

*Fix the system*, Kael had said, at fourteen.

Senna was thirty-two now. The same age as Marcus. Old enough to know that fixing a system meant understanding what it dropped, and who got hurt by the dropping, and what you were willing to carry so that the people who came after you wouldn't have to carry it alone.

She adjusted her cuff.

She went to work.
