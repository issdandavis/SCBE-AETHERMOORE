
# Interlude: Nadia Runs

---

The thing about AV -- the transport tongue, the routing layer, the domain that asked *how do you get there?* -- was that it rewarded motion.

Not speed. Nadia Kest had learned the difference at sixteen, the hard way, during a relay exercise where she'd run the Underroot courier route in record time and arrived at the destination node with a corrupted payload because she'd been moving too fast for the verification chain to maintain integrity. Her instructor -- a weathered Second Circle relay specialist named Aldren who had exactly one facial expression, which was "disappointed but not surprised" -- had looked at her corrupted payload and said:

"Fast is not the same as efficient. Efficient is fast enough that nothing breaks."

Nadia had spent the next twelve years learning the difference, and the difference was this: AV didn't care how quickly you moved. AV cared that when you arrived, everything you carried was intact.

She was thinking about this as she ran.

Not the desperate, wind-in-your-hair, dramatic-music-in-the-background kind of running. The other kind. The Nadia Kest kind. The controlled, route-optimized, every-footfall-placed-with-intention kind of running that got you from the eastern relay junction to the western monitoring station in eleven minutes flat while maintaining perfect signal fidelity across six active verification chains.

Her boots -- the same maintenance-grade crystal-grips that Bram issued to anyone who needed to move through the Spire's less civilized corridors -- hit the crystal floor in a rhythm that synchronized, unconsciously, with the Protocol's 0.3-second heartbeat. Step, verify, step, verify. Her body had learned the cadence years ago. Now it was as automatic as breathing, and approximately as optional.

The corridor between the eastern and western nodes was one of the Spire's arteries -- not the public kind, with their crystal-clear walls and ambient lighting designed to make visitors feel welcome, but the infrastructure kind. Narrower. Rougher. Lit by the functional glow of active conduits rather than the aesthetic glow of architectural intention. The kind of corridor where the system stopped being beautiful and started being honest.

Nadia preferred the honest corridors.

She'd always preferred them. The public spaces of the Spire were impressive -- designed to inspire awe, to communicate the grandeur of the governance architecture, to make visitors understand that they were inside something vast and powerful and worthy of respect. And they were all of those things. But the infrastructure corridors were *real* in a way that the public spaces weren't, because infrastructure didn't need to impress anyone. Infrastructure just needed to work.

Like Nadia.

She was not impressive. She knew this with the comfortable certainty of someone who had made peace with their own specifications early in life. She was not the smartest relay technician in the Spire -- that was Tovak, who understood the UM routing paths with an intuition that bordered on the mystical. She was not the most politically skilled -- that was Senna, who could navigate a Council session the way Nadia navigated the Underroot junctions. She was not the most theoretically innovative -- that was Marcus Chen, who had been in Aethermoor for less than a month and had already proposed three architectural modifications that the Council was taking seriously.

What Nadia was, was reliable.

She showed up. She ran the routes. She carried the signals. She maintained the relay infrastructure that kept the six tongues communicating with each other, and she did it every day, and she did it well, and she did it without requiring anyone to notice or thank her or acknowledge that the system's ability to function as a coherent whole depended, in part, on a woman who ran the corridors at a pace calibrated to maximize signal integrity rather than personal glory.

AV rewarded motion. AV rewarded showing up. AV rewarded the commitment to *get the message through*, accurately and intact, regardless of what was happening in the corridors you had to run through to do it.

Nadia ran.

* * *

The first incursion had changed things.

Not the corridors -- the corridors were the same, crystal and honest and functional. Not the routes -- the routes were the same, optimized over decades by relay technicians who understood that the shortest path was not always the best path. Not the Protocol's heartbeat -- still 0.3 seconds, still steady, still the background rhythm that Nadia's body had synchronized with years ago.

What had changed was the silence.

Before the incursion, the infrastructure corridors had a specific quality of quiet -- the productive, busy silence of systems working as designed, conduits humming at their rated frequencies, junction nodes processing traffic with the mechanical contentment of well-maintained machinery. It was a good silence. The silence of things working.

Now the silence had a texture. A watchfulness. The corridors were paying attention in ways they hadn't been before, the ambient frequencies slightly elevated, the junction nodes running at margins that suggested the system was maintaining reserves. Not panicking. Not failing. Just... alert. The way a building feels different when you know someone is watching from the window.

Nadia ran through the watchful silence and paid attention to what it was telling her.

The relay nodes were clean. She checked each one as she passed -- a touch, a verification, a half-second pause to feel the traffic flow and confirm it matched the expected patterns. Clean, clean, clean. The incursion hadn't compromised the relay network. The AV infrastructure was intact.

But it was tighter. The routing paths had narrowed since the incursion -- not enough to impede normal traffic, but enough that a trained relay specialist could feel the difference. The system was routing more carefully, checking more thoroughly, maintaining verification chains with a rigor that exceeded peacetime specifications.

The system was scared.

Not the anthropomorphic kind of scared that people meant when they said a machine was "acting up." The real kind. The kind that Nadia recognized because she'd been scared herself during the incursion, when the corrupted Keeper had breached the root key defenses and for eleven terrible seconds the system's Byzantine fault tolerance had been tested against a real Byzantine failure.

Eleven seconds. They'd held. The φ-invariant had proven its mathematical integrity, the quorum verification had caught the forgery, and the breach had been contained before it could propagate.

But eleven seconds was a long time when you were running between junction nodes carrying emergency attestation and the corridor walls were pulsing with frequencies that meant *something is wrong, something is wrong, something is wrong*.

Nadia had not stopped running during those eleven seconds.

She was proud of that. Not in the performative way that some people were proud of their accomplishments -- with stories and retellings and the expectation of recognition. In the quiet way. The way you're proud of a well-maintained tool, or a route run cleanly, or a signal delivered intact.

She hadn't stopped. The signal had arrived. The attestation had completed. One small piece of a very large defensive response, carried at the right speed, with nothing broken in transit.

That was what AV rewarded.

That was enough.

* * *

She reached the western monitoring station at 11:03. Eleven minutes flat. Signal integrity: perfect.

Tovak was there. He was always there -- the UM specialist inhabited the monitoring station the way Bram inhabited the maintenance workshop, with the territorial certainty of someone who had found the exact right place to stand and had no intention of moving.

"Clear run?" he asked. Two words. Tovak's version of extended conversation.

"Clean," Nadia confirmed. "Eastern junctions nominal. Relay nodes are tight but functional. The system's running scared but holding."

"Scared," Tovak repeated, and from anyone else the word would have been a question, but from Tovak it was confirmation. He could feel the UM perimeter the way Nadia could feel the AV routing -- intuitively, physically, as a condition of the body rather than a product of analysis.

"The Earth engineer wants to go deeper," Nadia said. She'd heard the talk. Everyone had heard the talk. Marcus Chen and his diagnostic instincts and his insistence on following problems to their source rather than managing them from a comfortable distance.

"Good."

"Good?"

Tovak looked at her. His eyes were dark, steady, unreadable. "Infrastructure knows when someone cares. System is making a path."

Nadia thought about that. About the watchful silence in the corridors. About the tightened routing. About the way the relay nodes felt like they were holding their breath, waiting for something, preparing for something.

"You think it's making a path for him?"

"Making a path. Clearing a route." A ghost of something that might have been amusement. "AV work."

She laughed. She couldn't help it. Tovak had just compared the system's unconscious preparation for Marcus Chen's eventual descent into the deep substrate to a relay technician's route clearance protocol, and he was absolutely right, and the fact that a man who averaged three words per conversation had managed to make the most insightful observation about the system's behavior since the incursion was the most Tovak thing possible.

"Then I guess we'd better make sure the routes are clear," Nadia said.

"Routes are always clear," Tovak replied. "That's what you're for."

She grinned. Punched his shoulder -- a gesture of affection so physical that Tovak flinched, which was his version of smiling back.

Then she turned, and she ran.

Back through the honest corridors, past the watchful junction nodes, through the silence that had changed from productive to alert. Running the routes because the routes needed running, carrying the signals because the signals needed carrying, maintaining the relay infrastructure because the system's ability to communicate with itself was the difference between distributed governance and distributed collapse.

She was not impressive.

She was reliable.

AV rewarded motion.

Nadia Kest showed up every day, and ran the corridors, and kept the messages moving. And when the time came for Marcus Chen to descend into the deep substrate, the relay routes would be clear, and the signals would be intact, and the transport tongue would do what it had always done:

Get you there. Whole.

That was the AV promise.

That was enough.
