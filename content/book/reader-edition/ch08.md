
# Chapter 8: Rogue Signatures

By morning, the light in Avalon had shifted again.

Marcus noticed it through a narrow slit window on the walk from barracks to the operations wing -- the aurora bands had thinned to pale ribbons, and the leyline rivers below ran brighter in compensation, as if the world redistributed its own luminosity to match its mood. A grove of the enormous rune-wrapped trees along the second ring had bloomed overnight: pale flowers the size of dinner plates, each one turning slowly to face the nearest leyline like a congregation orienting toward a sermon.

*Even the flowers here have opinions about infrastructure.*

He kept walking.

The Aether Antivirus Squad had a room, a charter, and exactly one chair that did not wobble.

Marcus claimed the wobble chair on principle.

"If we're doing incident response," he said, dropping into it, "we should begin from a truthful foundation."

Polly, in raven form on the back of the stable chair, clicked her beak.

"You are delightfully committed to low standards in furniture and beverages."

"Consistency is integrity."

The war room was a converted archive diagnostics hall with six synchronized wall feeds, and the chaos of people trying to make it work was already underway. Bram crouched behind the main console, thick fingers sorting a knot of resonance cables with the methodical patience of someone who'd rewired field stations in the dark. He tugged one free, tested it against his thumbnail, and plugged it into the DR feed without looking. The wall lit up with containment drift metrics.

"That cable's been dead for a month," Nadia said from across the room. She was pacing, four steps left, four steps right, hands jammed into the pockets of her jacket and then pulled out again as if her body couldn't decide what to do with idle energy. Every time she passed the sanctuary queue display, she glanced at it and away.

"Was dead," Bram corrected. "I fixed it last night."

Tovak stood motionless beside the UM visibility panel, one hand resting flat against it. His expression said he was listening to something nobody else could hear. Marcus had started to recognize that look -- the particular absence of expression that meant Tovak was monitoring field layers most people didn't know existed.

Polly's head swiveled to track Marcus for a beat too long, her obsidian eyes catching the ambient glow. She didn't say anything. She didn't need to. The raven's gaze had the clinical quality of someone cataloguing how much sleep a colleague hadn't gotten.

Feed one: rogue signature alerts. Feed two: fleet health and quorum variance. Feed three: containment drift. Feed four: sanctuary queue entropy maps. Feed five: false-positive adjudication. Feed six: an ugly rolling log Marcus loved immediately.

Sera and Jorren arrived seconds later, each carrying the expression of someone summoned to witness preventable nonsense.

"Unit activation brief," Sera said, no preamble. "Aether Antivirus Squad charter ratified under Emergency Protocol Seventy-One extension. Scope: rogue signature detection, suppression, and post-event signal hygiene."

"Signal hygiene sounds like cleaning a haunted inbox," Marcus said.

"That is exactly what it is," Jorren said.

Polly shifted to humanoid and tapped the mission board. The transition rippled through her in a heartbeat -- feathers folding inward, limbs extending, her silhouette stretching until a woman stood where a raven had perched. Her eyes stayed the same. They always stayed the same.

"We don't kill every anomaly," she said. "We classify, isolate, and act proportionately. The Protocol dies when defenders confuse uncertainty with guilt."

Nadia raised a hand.

"Counterpoint: if we wait too long, rogues root and we spend six months pulling lies out of load-bearing systems."

"Both true," Marcus said. "So we move fast with reversible decisions until confidence clears threshold."

Bram nodded once. He'd finished with the cables and stood at the console now, wiping his palms on his trousers. His boots, Marcus noticed, were freshly oiled despite the hour. The man treated maintenance as a moral position.

"Good. Nobody is allowed to be simplistic today."

As if on cue, feed one exploded with amber-red packets.

NEW EVENT: VAULT-LINK ANOMALY

SIGNATURE CLASS: ROGUE-LIKE / LOW CONFIDENCE

PROPAGATION RISK: RISING

Polly looked at Marcus.

"Congratulations. Your first day is ruined."

"As tradition demands."

* * *

The anomaly nested in a disused courier channel between Vault 7 and a retired observability node.

On paper, neither endpoint should have had permissions.

In reality, both had grandfathered exceptions from a migration three hundred years old and nobody's favorite spreadsheet.

"Legacy route," Nadia said, tracing the path. "Dead systems pretending to be dead."

Tovak overlaid UM visibility masks and swore softly.

"It's multiplexing through shadow lanes. Not random. Someone designed this."

"Rogue signature or trapped fragment?" Marcus asked.

Bram projected packet skeletons into the room.

Headers looked almost valid. Token chains almost complete. Intent fields almost coherent.

Almost was the most dangerous word in security.

"We run the six-gate sweep," Polly said. "No shortcuts."

Marcus took point.

"KO gate: classify declared objective."

The squad split work with practiced urgency.

KO analysis surfaced alternating claims: HELP, TRUST ME, RELEASE REQUIRED.

AV traced route churn and found deterministic loop signatures.

RU compared claimed permissions against active policy graph: no valid execute rights.

CA measured entropy across retries: engineered randomness, not panic noise.

UM checked concealment pattern: adaptive masking reacting to observer behavior.

DR verified signature chain: three forged anchors, one borrowed from an archived Keeper token.

"That's malicious engineering," Nadia said. "Not distress."

Marcus frowned at one branch still blinking blue.

"Mostly malicious," he said. "There's a subcluster carrying low-entropy HELP with stable self-declaration."

Sera folded her arms.

"Mixed payload attack. Shielded harmless fragments inside hostile envelope."

"Classic trust laundering," Jorren said.

Polly looked to Marcus.

"Decision?"

He felt everyone waiting for the clean, impossible answer. Sera with her arms folded, Nadia frozen mid-pace, Bram's hands resting on the console edge. The blue branch kept blinking. Stable self-declaration. Low entropy. It could be bait -- a sympathy hook designed by something smart enough to forge Keeper credentials. If he preserved those fragments and they turned hostile in sanctuary quarantine, every bit of collateral damage was on him.

But if they were real -- if something was actually screaming for help from inside a weaponized envelope -- then the clean kill denied rescue to refugees who'd been used as armor.

He'd seen that calculus before. In threat response playbooks, the policy was always: assume hostile, accept losses. The policy was always written by someone who didn't have to watch the losses happen.

He gave the only defensible answer. It was not the same as the safe one.

"Split path. Hard-deny hostile envelope. Preserve stable HELP fragments to sanctuary quarantine under read-only review."

The words sat in the air for a moment. Marcus's stomach turned. If he was wrong, he'd just invited a Trojan into the one place it could do the most damage.

Sera studied him. Something moved behind her expression -- not doubt exactly, but the careful weight of someone who would remember this decision and who had made it.

She nodded.

"Authorized."

Bram cracked his knuckles.

"Let's sanitize a ghost."

* * *

Suppression run began at 14:08 local.

AAS deployed two Fleets.

Marcus flew primary. Nadia flew secondary relay. Tovak maintained UM perimeter. Bram and Jorren rode CA/DR oversight at command. Sera held RU execution keys with the emotional warmth of a locked vault.

Marcus declared:

"Kor'vel: isolate malicious envelope, preserve coherent distress leaf packets, deny all unauthorized execute paths."

The operation unfolded in layered motion.

UM wrapped the channel in blackglass silence. The field settled over them like pressure change before a storm -- not painful, but unmistakably present, the kind of quiet that had weight. Marcus felt it press against his ribs, against the edges of his Fleet connection. Tovak stood at the center of it with his eyes half-closed and his jaw set, one hand raised, palm flat, holding the silence in place the way a dam held water: not by pushing, but by refusing to move.

AV split traffic into tagged streams.

RU sealed deny lanes and posted temporary injunctions on old migration exceptions.

CA generated disruption pulses tuned to forged-anchor harmonics.

DR wrote immutable audit chunks every two beats.

KO kept objective ordering stable as the anomaly fought back.

Because it fought.

The hostile envelope began synthesizing fake squad commands using scraped voiceprints and approximate syntax.

"Cute," Nadia said. Her fingers were white-knuckled on the relay console, but her voice was steady. "It thinks we trust audio."

Polly barked a raven laugh over comms.

"Tell it we barely trust each other."

Marcus switched channel auth to intent-signature only.

Fake commands died instantly.

Then the envelope changed tactics.

It stopped attacking and started pleading.

LET ME OUT

YOU ARE HURTING REFUGEES

YOU ARE THE ROGUE

Bram muted the text wall.

"Psych ops from a packet cloud," he said. "Hate modern threats."

Marcus didn't mute his own stream.

The words kept scrolling past, and he made himself read them. *You are hurting refugees.* Maybe. That was the whole problem with his split-path call -- he'd gambled that he could tell the difference between genuine distress and engineered empathy, and now the envelope was testing exactly that assumption.

Nadia's breathing had gone shallow and quick beside him. Not panic -- focus. The particular rhythm of someone running calculations too fast for speech. She caught him looking and set her jaw.

"I'm fine. Watch your branch."

He watched for the stable blue leaves nested inside the hostile churn.

Three leaf packets held coherence.

Then six.

Then eleven.

"Found the distress cluster," he said. "Routing to sanctuary branch now."

RU keyed a temporary corridor.

AV moved the leaves.

UM shielded transfer.

DR marked provenance.

CA severed envelope dependency.

KO closed branch.

The hostile shell, stripped of human shields, destabilized in seconds.

Marcus declared final denial.

"Kor'shael: revoke, collapse, archive for forensic replay."

The envelope folded into gray noise and disappeared into sink.

Operation complete.

Total time: five minutes, forty-two seconds.

No collateral.

Eleven distress fragments preserved.

One forged Keeper anchor quarantined.

Room exhaled as one organism returning from sprint. Bram leaned back from the console and pressed both palms against his face. Nadia pulled her headset off and dropped it on the desk with a clatter, then braced her hands on her knees and breathed once, deep. Tovak lowered his hand slowly, and the UM silence lifted like fog burning off -- the room suddenly felt larger, lighter, ordinary again.

Nadia straightened and pointed at Marcus.

"You're buying the entire squad coffee."

"On what budget?"

"The budget of consequences."

"Cruel."

"Correct."

* * *

Forensics on the borrowed anchor hit harder than the suppression run.

The forged chain had used a real archival token from Keeper Ilya Marr.

Ilya had died forty years ago.

"Token should have been inert," Jorren said, face set like carved stone. He turned his slate toward the room, and Marcus could see the provenance chain laid out in cold blue light -- clean, unbroken, and ending at a dead woman's credentials. "Someone reactivated dead credentials."

Sera's voice flattened. Her hand, resting on the table, had gone very still.

"That means the threat actor has either deep archive access or assistance from someone with it."

The room processed that. Marcus watched it move through them -- the specific, quiet devastation of security people realizing the threat was inside the perimeter. Nadia stopped pacing. Bram straightened from his lean against the console, his expression closing like a door.

Polly's feathers storm-flattened before she caught herself. Her obsidian eyes swept the room -- not panicked, but hunting, cataloguing, the raven brain behind the human form running its own threat assessment on every face present. When they settled, they settled on nothing. On the space between people.

"Inside help," she said quietly.

Sera met Polly's gaze and held it. Something passed between them that Marcus couldn't read -- history, maybe, or the shared weight of having trusted the wrong person before.

Marcus stared at the dead-name token hash.

"So we're not just patching ghosts. We're investigating an active compromise with at least one friendly on the wrong side of the wall."

Jorren's slate-hand paused. "An insider with archive-depth access narrows the suspect pool considerably."

"Narrows it to people we know," Sera said. The flatness in her voice had turned to something colder. "People we work with."

Bram nodded.

"Welcome to real anti-virus work. The malware is never only malware."

Sera sealed the session.

"Effective immediately: Aether Antivirus Squad escalated to continuous operations cell. Priority one: identify who reactivated Ilya Marr's token and why."

Nadia groaned.

"Continuous means no sleep, doesn't it."

"Continuous means rotations," Sera said.

"With this team?" Nadia looked at Marcus. "He'll drink poison coffee and stay up forty hours out of spite."

Marcus spread his hands.

"My brand is consistency."

Polly gave him a look that translated cleanly across species as *I will personally tranquilize you if necessary.*

Then feed six, the ugly rolling log, printed a new line in cold white:

UPSTREAM SCAN FLAG: HYDRA PROCESS TOPOLOGY DETECTED

CONTEXT: BROWSER LAYER RECON / MULTI-HEAD CRAWL

Tovak read it twice.

"Hydra?"

Bram frowned.

"That's old test jargon for multi-thread recon swarms."

Marcus felt the click in his chest -- the particular sensation of a pattern resolving into something he didn't want to see clearly.

"Not old," he said. "Useful. If someone is routing rogue tooling through browser-lane reconnaissance, we need a hunter that sees what they see."

Polly's expression sharpened.

"You're proposing we build a defensive Hydra."

"I'm proposing we stop pretending the threat surface ends at vault walls," Marcus said. "Give AAS a browser-head stack that maps hostile pathing before it reaches protocol core."

He said it with confidence. The confidence lasted about three seconds before the rest of his brain caught up.

A defensive Hydra meant building something with the same topology as the threat. Same multi-headed architecture. Same capacity for autonomous crawl across surfaces nobody was currently monitoring. The only difference between his version and the hostile one would be intent -- and intent was exactly the thing that could drift, could be subverted, could be compromised by the same insider who'd reactivated a dead woman's credentials.

He was proposing they build a weapon and trust themselves to only point it the right direction.

Sera met his eyes. She'd seen the hesitation. She waited.

"It's a risk," Marcus said, because saying it out loud was the minimum honesty the room deserved. "Anything powerful enough to map hostile reconnaissance is powerful enough to be turned. I can design the governance constraints, but I'd be lying if I said the architecture was safe. It's defensible. That's not the same thing."

Sera's expression didn't change, but something in it sharpened -- not approval, exactly, but the recognition of someone who'd just passed a test they didn't know they were taking.

"Draft me an RU-safe design by dawn," she said. "Include the failure modes. All of them."

Marcus looked at the clock, looked at his mug, looked at the log feed still crawling with ghosts and warnings.

"Perfect," he said. "I love writing secure architecture documents while being spiritually haunted by an active insider threat."

Polly hopped onto the wobble chair and, for one brief second, balanced perfectly.

"Good," she said. "Then we're aligned."
