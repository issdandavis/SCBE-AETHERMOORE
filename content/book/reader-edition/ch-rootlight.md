
# Epilogue: Rootlight

The festival arrived without asking permission, because festivals in Ravencrest did not require permits so much as consensus that it was time.

Marcus learned this when he walked into the citadel courtyard at dusk expecting dinner and found the entire space transformed by people who apparently treated decorating the same way his old team treated deployments: quietly, efficiently, and with aggressive opinions about where the lights should go.

Root-lights. Hundreds of them. Small living lanterns of amber and pale gold, each one grown from a splinter of the World Tree's outer bark and soaked in leyline water until the wood remembered how to glow. They hung from orchard branches, from iron hooks along the garden walls, from wires strung between the citadel towers. They did not flicker. They breathed. Each one pulsed in a slightly different rhythm, and together they produced a collective glow that made the stone look warm and the evening look intentional.

"Rootlight," Polly said from a branch above the courtyard gate, overseeing the preparations with the expression of someone who was absolutely not orchestrating the event and was merely observing it with coincidental precision. "Annual observance. Roads blessed. Names spoken. Gratitude expressed. Food consumed in inadvisable quantities."

"You organized this."

"*Caw!*" She fluffed her feathers with theatrical modesty. "I facilitated conditions under which organization became inevitable." She preened one wing. "Different skill."

Marcus watched a line of children carrying lanterns between waystones along the outer road, their faces lit from below in colors that shifted with their footsteps. Each child walked slowly and deliberately, holding the light out in front of them with both hands like something precious and temporary.

"What are they doing?"

"Teaching the roads their current voices." Polly tilted her head. "Rootlight remembers that paths carry names. When you walk a leyline road with a living light and speak your own name aloud, the road updates its registry. Current voice. Current intention. Current self."

Marcus thought about the roadstone that morning. The girl who had been sent to her dead grandmother. The path that had confused loyalty with direction.

"This is maintenance disguised as celebration," he said.

"All good celebrations are. The ones that are only celebration never last."

* * *

Alexander Thorne arrived through the garden gate carrying six root-lights in a wire frame and a coil of copper-threaded cord over one shoulder. He moved like someone who had been doing physical work all afternoon and considered this preferable to whatever administrative alternative had been offered.

"Chen," he said.

"Thorne," Marcus replied, testing the formality.

Alexander's mouth twitched. The same Izack-adjacent smile Marcus had seen in the gallery portrait, except warmer because it was alive and slightly tired and accompanied by actual dirt on his cuffs.

"Help me hang these before Lyra decides the spacing is wrong and recalculates everything." He offered Marcus three lights and the cord. "She has done this four times already today."

"Is her spacing wrong?"

"Her spacing is mathematically optimal and aesthetically violent. We are going for warm." Alexander started walking toward the inner garden wall. "You know how to climb a ladder and hold things simultaneously?"

"I was an engineer. Multi-object suspension is literally my job."

"Good. Orchard women will correct your posture. Accept it gracefully."

Marcus followed, and within two minutes found himself halfway up a stone-braced ladder in the inner garden, threading copper cord through iron hooks while Alexander held the base steady and handed up lights one by one.

The work was simple. Hold the cord. Hang the light. Move. Hold the cord. Hang the light. Move.

Marcus discovered, approximately four lights in, that Alexander Thorne was easier to talk to than anyone he had met in Aethermoor.

Not because Alexander was simple. He wasn't. Behind the steady posture and the unhurried voice was a mind that ran calculations at a speed Marcus recognized from the best engineers he'd worked with on Earth -- people who processed so fast they could afford to be calm, because they had already considered the five worst outcomes and rejected three of them before the conversation reached them.

But where Senna's calm was load-bearing and Polly's was institutional and Sera's was judicial, Alexander's was generous. He left space. He did not fill silence with authority. He asked questions and then listened with his whole body, which was the rarest trait Marcus had ever encountered in anyone who held power.

"How are you finding it?" Alexander asked. Not *how are you doing.* Not *how do you feel about the existential weight of defending reality from a patient god.* Just -- how are you finding it.

"Complicated," Marcus said. "Good. Complicated and good."

"That's the honest version."

"You can tell?"

"My father used to say that if you want to know whether someone is answering truthfully, listen to whether they say the hard part first." Alexander steadied a light that Marcus had hung slightly crooked. "You said complicated before good. That's honest."

Marcus adjusted the cord and reached for the next light.

"Your father was a systems architect who built a world. Did he also do festival lighting?"

Alexander laughed. Quiet and real.

"Every year. He said the lights were the only infrastructure he could build where nobody argued about governance." He paused. "That was not true. Polly argued about governance with the lights at least twice. But the sentiment held."

They worked in silence for a minute. Below, the courtyard filled with people. Not the Spire's institutional population -- these were Viremont locals. Orchard workers, road wardens, field hands, a cluster of students from the boundary school. Women set up long tables under the garden trees. A man with a barrel the size of Marcus's ambitions rolled it into position near the gate and began tapping it with the seriousness of someone performing surgery.

"Can I ask you something?" Marcus said.

"You already have. But continue."

"Senna carries the weight of -- everything. Continuity, Council, Kael, the family. You're the eldest. Why is it on her?"

Alexander looked up at him from the base of the ladder. Not defensive. Not surprised. The expression of someone who had asked himself the same question for a very long time and arrived at an answer he did not enjoy.

"Because I chose the outer ring," he said. "Floating Shelves. Fleet defense. Distance. Senna chose to stay. Lyra chose motion. Mira chose silence. I chose the perimeter because I was good at it and because staying in the Spire after what happened to Kael would have turned my grief into something structural." He met Marcus's eyes. "Senna carries the weight because she decided someone had to, and then she did not put it down."

"That's not fair."

"No." Alexander's voice was quiet. "But she would tell you that fairness and necessity share a border and necessity has better maps."

Marcus hung the last light. Climbed down. Stood beside Alexander in the warm glow of the garden wall and realized that the eldest Thorne child was not the dutiful one or the prophetic one or the golden one. He was the one who had looked at the family's fracture and chosen the exit that hurt least while hating himself for the calculus that made him choose it.

"She's better at it than I would have been," Alexander said, watching the courtyard fill. "That doesn't mean I don't know the cost."

A woman with grey braids and an apron came past, looked at the root-lights on the inner wall, pointed at two that were slightly too high, and said, "Lower. The children need to reach them."

Marcus adjusted them.

"Thank you," the woman said. Then, to Alexander: "Your mother is in the west garden. She wanted to show the Earth one the tree."

Alexander nodded. He looked at Marcus.

"Go," he said. "When Aria wants to tell you something, it's because she's decided you're ready for it."

"Ready for what?"

"For the part of the family you haven't been told yet." Alexander clapped his shoulder once. "And eat something before she starts. Mother forgets that mortals need food during emotional history lessons."

* * *

The west garden was quieter than the courtyard. Fewer lanterns. More tree.

The World Tree rose above the inner walls like something that had outgrown its original purpose and decided to keep going anyway. Its silver-white trunk glowed faintly in the dusk, living script moving through the bark in slow currents. Its branches held canopy enough to shade a small village, and the iridescent leaves turned in no wind, turning toward sounds and intentions the way sunflowers tracked light.

Aria Ravencrest Thorne stood beneath it with one palm flat to the bark.

She was smaller than Marcus expected. Or maybe not smaller -- more concentrated. Everything about her suggested a person who had spent centuries eliminating waste motion and unnecessary volume. Her auburn hair was threaded with silver now, braided with copper that caught root-light in amber sparks. She wore gardening clothes, not ceremony. Practical shoes. Dirt under two fingernails.

She did not look like the wife of the man who had built a world.

She looked like the reason the world had stayed standing after he left.

"Marcus Chen," she said without turning.

"Lady Thorne."

"Aria." She looked at him over her shoulder. "I don't use titles in this garden. The tree finds them pretentious."

Marcus glanced at the trunk.

"The tree has opinions about social protocol?"

"The tree has opinions about everything. It is the most judgmental organism I have ever met, and I was married to an elf who argued with furniture." She removed her hand from the bark and gestured for Marcus to walk beside her.

They moved along a gravel path that wound through the lower roots. The tree's root system extended farther than Marcus could see, diving through the garden wall and threading into the bedrock below. Some roots glowed. Some didn't. All of them carried the faint hum of infrastructure operating at a scale that made the Spire's engineering look like a prototype.

"You wanted to show me something," Marcus said.

"I wanted to tell you something," Aria said. "Showing is just how trees do it."

She stopped at a point where three roots converged around a flat stone. The stone was worn smooth by what Marcus estimated to be several centuries of people sitting on it.

"Sit," Aria said.

He sat.

She didn't.

"When Izack and I came to this garden," she said, "the World Tree was a sapling. Three feet tall. Silver bark, no leaves, no script. The Ravencrest family had been trying to make it bloom for eleven generations."

"Why couldn't they?"

"Because they treated it like a problem. A thing to be solved. Fed, watered, warded, analyzed, debated over, prayed at." Aria's voice was not warm. It was precise. The voice of a boundary architect describing a boundary she had understood before anyone else.

"My father tried alchemy. My grandfather tried blood rites. My great-grandmother tried singing to it for a decade. She had a lovely voice. The tree was unimpressed."

"What did Izack do?"

Aria looked at him with Senna's eyes and Alexander's stillness and a patience that predated all of them.

"He sat where you're sitting. And I sat beside him. And we argued about ward geometry for three hours because he had an idea about nested trust boundaries that I told him was elegant and structurally reckless, which was true on both counts."

She paused.

"Somewhere around hour two, we stopped arguing about the wards and started arguing about what trust means when you can't verify it. Whether vulnerability is a security flaw or a feature. Whether two people disagreeing productively is stronger than one person being right alone."

Marcus felt the back of his neck prickle.

"And the tree bloomed."

"In the middle of a sentence." Aria's mouth curved. Not a smile. A memory wearing the shape of one. "Izack was saying something about how the six tongues only work because they disagree, and I was telling him that disagreement without stakes is just conversation, and the tree produced its first leaf. One leaf. Silver-green with script we couldn't read yet."

"What did the script say?"

"We didn't find out for thirty years." Aria sat on the root beside the stone and looked up at the canopy. "When we finally developed the translation, the first leaf said: *Neither of you was right. Both of you were necessary.*"

The tree's bark pulsed once, as if confirming.

A warm breeze moved through the garden. Not from the sky. From the roots. From the bark. From the substrate itself, where a tired man sat in a crystal chair holding twenty-one dimensions together, and the breeze carried the faintest scent of honeyed mint and apple cider -- Fizzlecress smoke, drifting upward through five hundred years of living wood like a breath that had never quite finished exhaling.

Marcus felt it on his face. The same warmth he'd felt through the kitchen window in San Francisco, a lifetime ago. The same warmth that had risen through his chest when the Protocol asked *what do you intend* and his honesty had answered before his words could. *Kor'thul sil'nav.* The heart-spiral that grows through connection. He didn't know the phrase yet. But his body knew the frequency.

Aria's hand rested on the root. The bark answered her differently than it answered anyone else Marcus had seen touch it -- warmer, more specific, the response of something that recognized her not as a visitor but as a conversation it had never stopped carrying.

"He's still here," Aria said. Not sadly. The way an engineer states a load-bearing fact. "After Kael, the architecture began to fracture. The tongues drifted. The world-substrate lost coherence in places it could not afford to lose. Izack understood the load better than anyone alive -- better than anyone who has ever lived -- and he made a decision."

She paused.

"He bound himself into the Tree. Into the Spire. Into the substrate beneath both. Not to die. Not to ascend. To hold." Her voice carried the precision of someone who had spent decades refusing to let other people turn her husband's sacrifice into mythology. "He is present. He is load-bearing. He is not available for dinner."

Marcus looked at the trunk. At the script flowing through it. At the pulse that had answered Aria's touch with the specificity of recognition.

"The system carries his will," he said.

"The system carries his weight," Aria corrected. "His will is what keeps the weight from becoming collapse."

Marcus looked at the branches. At the leaves still turning. At the script flowing through centuries-old bark, still running, still carrying the argument that had started it.

"It bloomed for collaboration," he said. "Not for devotion. Not for power. For two people arguing productively."

"For two people who trusted each other enough to disagree in front of something that was listening." Aria looked at him directly. "The system my husband built -- the tongues, the verification, the distributed consensus -- it wasn't born from theory. It was born from this." She touched the root beside her. "From learning that the strongest structure isn't the one that never argues. It's the one that argues well and holds anyway."

From the courtyard beyond the wall, Marcus heard music beginning. Clayborn's instrument -- the deep, resonant tone that made root-gold answer. And layered on top, other sounds: drums, a pipe, voices lifting into something that wasn't quite a song and wasn't quite a prayer and was probably both.

Aria stood.

"Come," she said. "The festival doesn't wait for people who are busy being moved. That's a policy Polly insisted on."

* * *

The courtyard at full Rootlight was the most alive place Marcus had ever seen.

Long tables ran the length of the garden in crossed rows, covered in food that ranged from recognizable (bread, fruit, roasted things that smelled like home even if home was no longer the right word) to entirely alien. Marcus tried the blue soup first. It tasted like blueberries, which was reassuring, and then like the memory of blueberries, which was not — a cool sweetness that dissolved on his tongue and left behind something older and stranger, like tasting the color itself, the way a cold lake tastes blue before you realize what you're thinking. His chest loosened. His eyes watered. He set the bowl down and didn't pick it up again.

The pastry was worse. Or better. It changed flavor with each bite — the first was warm bread and salted butter, the second was something citrus and bright that stung the roof of his mouth, the third was his grandmother's sesame oil and he almost dropped it because the taste arrived with the memory and the memory arrived with the grief and the grief arrived with the knowledge that the pastry had not done this on purpose. It was just responding to what he was carrying. Marcus ate the rest of it quickly, before it could find anything else.

Somewhere in the courtyard, a child laughed at the soup's effect on their parent's face, and Marcus thought: *this is what it tastes like when a place decides you belong to it before you've decided to stay.*

A drink served in root-bark cups tasted like warm honey cut with something that made his teeth hum. Marcus tried it, winced, took another sip, and finished the cup before admitting to anyone that he liked it.

Nadia appeared from nowhere, face flushed, hair wild, carrying two cups of the root-bark drink and a grin that suggested she had been treating the entire festival as a competitive sport.

"Chen. You're drinking the bloom liquor."

"Under protest."

"You've had three cups."

"Consistent protest."

Bram stood near the gate, arms crossed, inspecting the root-lights with the expression of a man who trusted no infrastructure he hadn't personally stress-tested.

"These are wired to live roots," he said to no one in particular. "If one fails, it pulls current from the tree. That's a dependencies nightmare."

"It's a festival, Bram," Nadia said.

"Festivals with single-point-of-failure lighting are just darkness with better marketing."

Clayborn played near the World Tree, seated on a low bench, the instrument angled against the roots so its resonance fed directly into the wood. The sound moved through the courtyard like weather -- not background music but atmospheric. When Clayborn shifted keys, the tree's bark script changed rhythm to match. When the melody slowed, children stopped running and listened without being told.

Marcus watched from a table near the orchard wall. Polly perched on the branch above him, maintaining the fiction of non-involvement while occasionally muttering adjustments to a runner who kept appearing at her side.

"You're running this," Marcus said.

"I am allowing things to occur in a sequence I find acceptable."

"That's running it."

"Semantics."

A roadstone blessing began at the garden's southern edge. Families lined up along a low root-path, each one carrying a child holding a living light. One by one, the children stepped up to a small stone pillar, held their lanterns forward, and spoke their names aloud.

"Amara Linet Greenfall."

"Tomas Hirth."

"Jessa Rae Willowbank."

After each name, the adults around them repeated it. Not in unison -- in staggered witness, each voice arriving a half-beat after the last, so the name was confirmed not once but several times from several directions.

The road answered. Each time a name was spoken in distributed witness, the leyline stones along the courtyard's edge pulsed once -- faint, warm, acknowledging.

Marcus glanced at Senna. She was standing near the roadstone pillar, watching the children speak their names with an expression he hadn't seen before — not composure, not assessment, not the controlled stillness she wore in Council. Something softer. Her left hand had drifted from the cuff to her abdomen, resting there without apparent intention, the way hands rest on things they are thinking about without the brain's permission. She noticed him looking and the hand returned to her side, but the expression stayed a moment longer — the particular brightness of someone seeing a future they hadn't let themselves imagine yet.

Marcus filed this under the growing category of things about Senna that he couldn't explain and had stopped trying to.

Marcus understood then that the festival was not separate from what they had done that morning. It was the same thing at a different scale. The roadstone fix had been emergency surgery. This was the routine maintenance that kept the patient alive between crises.

*Huh,* Marcus thought. *So that's what maintenance looks like when you don't call it maintenance.*

He thought about Earth. About how identity there was a database entry, a government number, a password hash. Something you proved to machines in order to access services. Nobody gathered in a garden to speak your name so the roads would remember your current voice.

Nobody had to, because the roads on Earth didn't listen.

Clayborn's music shifted to something lower, older. A melody that Marcus didn't recognize but felt like recognition anyway -- the particular ache of hearing beauty in a place that wasn't supposed to be yours but had somehow become the only place that fit.

*I used to think home was a coordinate. A latitude. An address that received mail. Now I'm standing in a garden listening to a golem play a song that makes a tree remember names, and it feels more real than anything in my apartment ever did.*

*My apartment. Not home. My apartment.*

*When did I stop calling it home?*

He was still sitting with that thought when he noticed he was looking for someone.

Not Polly. Not Bram. Not Alexander or Nadia or Aria.

Senna.

He was scanning the courtyard the way he used to scan server logs -- pattern-matching for a specific signature in a field of noise. Except the noise was lantern light and laughter and children carrying their names through the evening, and the signature was a woman with load-bearing composure and Izack's jaw and the rarest smile in threshold country.

She was near the gate, talking to two orchard wardens. Off-duty in the sense that she was not holding Council sigils, not off-duty in any other sense -- even here, people came to her with questions, with reports, with the small frictions that accumulated in any community and found their way to the person who could be trusted to hold them without breaking.

But she was laughing.

Not the careful half-expression Marcus had seen in Council. An actual laugh. Quiet, brief, surprised out of her by something one of the wardens said. It transformed her face for exactly two seconds -- the composure cracking not into vulnerability but into the person the composure was built to protect.

Marcus looked away before she caught him looking.

Too late.

Lyra materialized beside him with the stealth of a probability researcher who had calculated the optimal moment for maximum embarrassment.

"You're staring," she said.

"I'm observing."

"You're staring with intent. That's different from observing and considerably more interesting."

"I was looking at the gate."

"The gate is not blushing. You are."

Marcus drank from his empty cup. Lyra watched this with undisguised satisfaction.

"For what it's worth," she said, quieter now, the teasing receding into something genuine, "she came tonight because you're here. She doesn't attend Rootlight anymore. Hasn't for years. Too much family memory in the light."

Marcus looked at Lyra.

"Why are you telling me this?"

"Because my sister has been holding everything together since our brother tried to tear it apart, and she has not once in forty years allowed anyone close enough to help her carry it." Lyra's emerald companion shifted on her shoulder. "And because she laughed just now, and I haven't heard her laugh at Rootlight since before the erasure."

She stood, patted his shoulder with entirely too much force, and disappeared into the crowd before he could respond.

* * *

The festival wound down the way good evenings do -- not with a final event but with a gradual softening. Tables cleared. Children fell asleep against parents. Root-lights dimmed from gold to ember. Clayborn's music became so quiet it was barely distinguishable from the tree's own hum.

Marcus stood at the garden wall, looking out over the darkened orchard country. Leyline roads traced faint gold lines through the landscape like veins in a living thing. The Mirrorthorne caught the last root-light and held it, a river carrying the memory of an evening it had no reason to keep and kept anyway.

Senna appeared beside him. Not suddenly. Not with the institutional precision of her Council arrivals. She just walked over and stood where there was space, shoulder to shoulder, looking at the same view.

They didn't speak for a while.

The Protocol pulsed. Point-three seconds. Check. But here, outside the Spire, in the warm aftermath of a festival that was also infrastructure maintenance, the pulse felt less like a metronome and more like a heartbeat. Not his. Not hers. The world's.

"Thank you for coming tonight," Marcus said.

"Lyra told you I don't usually attend."

"Lyra told me several things. Some of them were even appropriate."

Senna's mouth did the small tilt that Marcus had learned to watch for.

"My sister has no concept of conversational boundaries."

"She has an excellent concept. She just doesn't respect them."

"Accurate."

A root-light near them dimmed, then brightened, then dimmed again -- the last pulse before dormancy, like a candle deciding whether to go out.

"Aria told me about the first blooming," Marcus said. "The tree answering because two people argued well enough."

"My mother tells that story to people she's decided are important."

"Important how?"

Senna turned her head just enough to look at him. In the root-light, her eyes held something that wasn't composure and wasn't warmth and was somehow both at once, the way the Protocol was simultaneously verification and trust.

"Important to the family," she said.

The root-light went out.

Another one, farther along the wall, stayed lit a little longer.

Marcus had started the evening watching the roads.

At some point, without deciding to, he had started watching for Senna instead.

He didn't say this. He didn't need to. The tree behind them, the one that bloomed for collaboration and recorded arguments in living script, turned one leaf toward them both and held it there, as if taking notes.

"Walk me back?" Senna asked.

"Yes," Marcus said.

They walked through the quiet garden, through the last of the root-light, through the gate where the wardens nodded and the road hummed and the evening kept the kind of records that no DR ledger would ever need to formalize.

Behind them, Polly watched from the highest branch of the World Tree and said nothing at all.

Which, for Polly, was the most eloquent endorsement she had ever given.

* * *

They came back to the tree.

Not that night. Weeks later. After the cascade and the descent and Izack's pipe smoke and the seventh silence and all the things that the story would eventually record as architecture but that Marcus and Senna experienced as living through the hardest month of their lives while holding each other's hands in corridors that smelled like ozone and relief.

They came back to the garden because Senna asked to.

It was evening. The bark script moved slowly in the fading light — unhurried, contemplative, the handwriting of a system that had spent five hundred years learning how to be patient. The iridescent leaves turned toward them as they approached, not in surveillance but in recognition. The tree knew who they were. It had been taking notes since Rootlight.

Senna stopped at the stone where Aria had sat. The same worn stone where eleven generations of Ravencrest women had tried to make the tree bloom. The stone where Izack had sat and argued about ward geometry until the tree decided they were worth listening to.

She took Marcus's hand and placed it on her stomach.

She didn't say anything.

Marcus went still. The kind of still that happens when every process in a system pauses at once — everything in him paused.

Point-three seconds. Check.

Point-three seconds. Check.

Point-three seconds — and a third pulse, faint, barely there, nested inside the other two like an echo that had decided to become original signal.

"Something is growing," Senna said.

Marcus's hand didn't move. His breathing changed — deeper, slower, the breathing of a man whose body was processing what his mind hadn't caught up to yet.

"My mother told me once," Senna said, looking at the tree, "that my father planted a seed here and said it was for protection. And my mother said 'most would bring flowers.'"

The bark script shifted. Not alarmed. Not diagnostic. The same pale gold as the maintenance rune in sublevel four — the one that had lit up the first time Marcus built something with honest intent. The system noticing. The architecture leaning in.

"Senna," Marcus said. His voice cracked on her name the way it had cracked in the corridor — not from weakness but from the structural failure that happens when a container encounters something larger than its rated capacity.

"I know," she said. "The timing is—"

"Perfect."

She looked at him.

"The timing is perfect," he said. "Everything important in my life has happened at the wrong time. The white void at three in the morning. The kiss after an incursion. The love confession on a floor that smelled like burned cinnamon." His hand pressed gently against her stomach. "This is right on schedule."

Senna's mouth did the thing. The small tilt. The composure cracking in the right direction. But this time the crack went deeper and what came through was not composure or control or the person the armor was built to protect.

What came through was joy.

Joy. The plain kind. The kind that just shows up.

Above them, in the highest branch of the World Tree, Polly made one sound.

Not a word. Not a caw. Not the sharp click of professional assessment or the soft raven-mutter of reluctant affection.

The sleep-caw.

The soft, raspy vocalization she made when something was safe enough to close her eyes.

The tree's bark script pulsed once — gold, steady, warm — and the living text that had been recording the arguments of lovers and architects and boundary-keepers for five hundred years added one more line that neither Marcus nor Senna would ever read, because the tree's records were not for the people inside the story.

They were for the story itself.

And somewhere deep in the substrate, in a crystal chair that had held him for thirty-eight years, a tired man felt something change in the system's emotional weather. Not a cascade. Not a breach. Not a correction or a warning.

A new verification.

Three heartbeats where there had been two.

Izack Thorne did not look up from his work.

But his pipe smoke curled upward through five hundred years of living wood and emerged into the evening air of the garden where his daughter sat with the man who had fallen through the floor of one world and landed in hers, and the smoke carried the scent of honeyed mint and apple cider and the quiet, unglamorous, irreplaceable warmth of a grandfather who had felt the signal before anyone spoke the words.

The World Tree bloomed.

Not a leaf. Not a shift in the script. A bloom — one flower, silver-white, opening at the branch nearest them with the slow, deliberate grace of something that had been waiting for exactly this.

The first bloom in forty years.

Marcus looked up at it. Senna looked up at it. The flower held its petals open like a hand that had finally unclenched.

"Neither of you was right," Marcus said quietly, remembering Aria's story. "Both of you were necessary."

Senna leaned against him. His arm went around her. The flower glowed above them in the gathering dark, and the tree held its note, and the Protocol pulsed its patient, immovable heartbeat through the world it kept alive — three-tenths of a second, check, three-tenths of a second, check — and for the first time in its five-hundred-year history, the check returned a count it had never returned before.

Three. Verified. Present.

Growing.
