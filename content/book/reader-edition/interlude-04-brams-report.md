
# Interlude: Bram's Report

---

**INCIDENT REPORT — INTERNAL USE ONLY**

**Filed by**: Bram Cortez, CA Maintenance Lead, Infrastructure Division
**Date**: [Protocol-local timestamp, 14 days post-arrival of Earth-native designate CHEN, M.]
**Classification**: Routine — Personal Observation — Not For Council Review
**Status**: Filed to /dev/null because I'm writing this for me, not for Jorren's bloody archive

---

**Subject**: Assessment of Earth-native Engineer Marcus Chen, Re: Fitness for Continued Integration and Whether He's Going to Break Something Important

---

Right. So.

Fourteen days. Two weeks of the Earth man walking around the Spire touching things and asking questions and making the infrastructure do things it hasn't done in years because nobody else bothered to ask it nicely. Two weeks of watching a systems engineer treat a five-hundred-year-old magical governance architecture the way I treat a clogged conduit: with respect, curiosity, and the assumption that it's doing its best under difficult circumstances.

I don't write reports. Jorren writes reports. Jorren writes reports the way normal people breathe -- automatically, constantly, with a vague sense that stopping would be fatal. I maintain things. I fix things. I crawl through maintenance shafts at three in the morning with a wrench and a headlamp and a profound distrust of anyone who thinks governance happens in council chambers instead of pipe junctions.

But I'm writing this one because I need to put something down before it gets lost in the noise, and the noise is getting louder, and Marcus Chen is the first person in a decade who's noticed the noise is *saying something*.

**Observation 1: He Listens to Infrastructure**

Day three. I'm in the Level Six junction realigning a CA conduit that's been drifting off-resonance for months -- nothing critical, just the kind of slow degradation that happens when maintenance budgets get allocated to visible problems instead of invisible ones. Standard. Annoying. My problem, not anyone else's.

Chen walks in. Doesn't announce himself. Doesn't ask what I'm doing. Just stands in the doorway and listens.

"That conduit sounds tired," he says.

I almost drop my wrench.

*Tired.* Not "malfunctioning." Not "degraded." Not "requiring recalibration to specification parameters," which is what the last CA inspector said before filing a report that went directly into Jorren's archive and directly out of anyone's actionable awareness. Tired. Like the conduit was a living thing doing its best, which -- and I will deny this under formal interrogation -- is closer to the truth than most people in this Spire would be comfortable admitting.

"How long since it's had a full service?" Chen asks.

"Seven months."

"What's the recommended interval?"

"Three months."

He doesn't say anything else. Doesn't lecture me about maintenance schedules or resource allocation or the importance of infrastructure investment. Just nods, makes a note in that journal he carries everywhere, and leaves.

The next morning, I find a requisition form on my desk. Signed by Senna Thorne. Authorizing a full CA conduit service cycle for Levels Four through Eight. Priority: Elevated.

I ask Senna about it. She says Chen mentioned the conduit resonance issue during his morning briefing and suggested that infrastructure fatigue might be masking deeper system behaviors.

*Infrastructure fatigue might be masking deeper system behaviors.*

That's the most accurate sentence anyone has spoken about my job in eleven years.

**Observation 2: He Drinks Bad Coffee and Doesn't Complain About the Right Things**

The man misses coffee. This is well-documented. Polly tried to explain essence brew to him and he drank it with the expression of someone who has accepted that the universe is cruel but is choosing to survive regardless.

What he doesn't complain about: the sleeping arrangements, the food, the fundamental ontological horror of existing in a reality governed by cryptographic protocol architecture. What he does complain about: when tools aren't where they should be, when documentation doesn't match observed behavior, and when people use the word "fine" to describe systems that are clearly running on accumulated technical debt.

These are the correct things to complain about. I like this man.

**Observation 3: He Understands Maintenance**

Day nine. I'm explaining the RU conduit routing to Nadia, who is a fine relay technician but thinks infrastructure is something that happens to other people. Chen is sitting in the corner of the workshop, ostensibly reviewing Fleet training materials, actually listening to every word I say.

"You've got redundant routing through the Level Three junction," he says, without looking up. "If the primary path goes down, the failover adds eleven milliseconds of latency. That's fine for normal traffic, but if you're running emergency governance attestation through that path during a breach event, eleven milliseconds might mean the difference between a valid witness chain and a temporal mismatch."

I stare at him.

"I read the routing tables last night," he says. "Couldn't sleep. Figured I'd learn the plumbing."

*Couldn't sleep. Figured I'd learn the plumbing.*

There are Keepers who've been here for decades who don't understand the routing tables. Who think infrastructure is a solved problem, a foundation you stand on without thinking about, the way you stand on ground without thinking about geology. Chen spent his insomnia reading conduit specifications because he understood, instinctively and correctly, that the system's health started at the pipes.

I fixed the redundant routing the next morning. The failover now adds three milliseconds. Chen noticed the improvement and said "nice" without breaking stride on his way to breakfast.

"Nice."

That's the best compliment I've received in my career.

**Observation 4: His Boots Are Wrong**

This is going to sound irrelevant. It isn't.

Chen arrived in Earth footwear. Some kind of flat-soled office shoe designed for carpeted floors and the polite fiction that humans don't actually need to move their feet. He's been walking the Spire's crystal corridors in these shoes for two weeks, and I can hear him coming from three junctions away because the soles have no grip and he compensates by walking harder than he needs to.

The maintenance shafts have crystal floors. The junction rooms have crystal floors. The deep infrastructure corridors -- the ones he's going to need to walk if the Council sends him where I think they're going to send him -- have crystal floors that are sometimes wet, sometimes angled, and always unforgiving of people who slip.

He needs proper boots. The thick-soled, crystal-grip kind that the maintenance crew wears. The kind that let you walk confidently through spaces that weren't designed for casual visitors.

I've requisitioned a pair. Size matched from his Protocol intake assessment. Standard maintenance issue, because they're the best boots in the Spire and because the people who do the real work should have the tools they need.

I'll leave them outside his quarters tonight. No note. If he's the kind of person I think he is, he won't need one.

**Observation 5: He's Going Deeper**

This isn't official. This isn't sanctioned. This is Bram Cortez, maintenance lead, writing something down because he's been maintaining infrastructure for twenty-three years and he knows what it looks like when the system is preparing for someone to go deeper.

The conduits are cleaner than they should be. The routing paths are more efficient than they were last month. The maintenance shafts that lead to the deep infrastructure corridors -- the ones I've only walked twice in my career, the ones that go down to where the crystal stops pretending to be architecture and starts being honest about what it is -- those shafts have been subtly clearing themselves. Not dramatically. Not obviously. Just... the accumulated debris of decades shifting aside, the access panels loosening, the way a house settles its creaky floorboards when it knows company is coming.

The system is making a path.

The system is making a path for Marcus Chen.

I don't know where it leads. I have my suspicions, and my suspicions involve a crystal observatory and a tired man with a pipe and a relationship with this architecture that goes deeper than anyone living can fully understand. But suspicions aren't evidence, and Bram Cortez doesn't put suspicions in reports.

What I will put in this report, which is not a report, which is filed to nowhere, which exists only because sometimes you need to write a thing down so it's real:

Marcus Chen is the first person since Kael Thorne who looks at infrastructure and sees something that deserves care. The difference is that Kael wanted to rebuild the infrastructure from scratch, and Marcus wants to help it heal.

There's a gap in this system. Everybody knows it. Nobody says it. The dead get dropped and the living compensate and the pipes carry the extra load because that's what pipes do, because that's what infrastructure does, because the whole point of maintenance is to keep things working so well that nobody has to think about the fact that they're working.

Chen thinks about it.

Chen thinks about the conduits, the routing tables, the verification chains, the accumulated weight of five hundred years of undocumented compromises. He thinks about the things the system carries without acknowledgment, the same way he thinks about stale coffee and missing tools and boots that don't grip the floor properly.

He thinks about maintenance. Not as a lesser function. Not as the unglamorous work that supports the glamorous work. As the work itself. The real work. The work that keeps the system breathing.

I like this man.

I hope the boots fit.
