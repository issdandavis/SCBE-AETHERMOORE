# Counting Rhymes of Avalon

*Based on: One Two Buckle My Shoe, Five Little Monkeys, Ten in the Bed, This Old Man, Hickory Dickory Dock*
*Sacred Tongue: ALL SIX — each rhyme teaches one tongue*
*Ages: 3-6*

---

## Rhyme 1: Counting the Tongues

*(To be sung while clapping — one clap per line)*

**Pattern: Counting 1-6, each number paired with a tongue**

> One tongue, KO, say what you *mean*,
> *(clap)* Kor'aelin — the sharpest word you've seen.
>
> Two tongues, AV, carry it *far*,
> *(clap)* Avali — the traveling star.
>
> Three tongues, RU, bind it *tight*,
> *(clap)* Runethic — the oath of right.
>
> Four tongues, CA, make it *think*,
> *(clap)* Cassisivadan — the missing link.
>
> Five tongues, UM, hide it *deep*,
> *(clap)* Umbroth — the secret you keep.
>
> Six tongues, DR, prove it *true*,
> *(clap)* Draumric — the hammer that rings for you.
>
> Six Sacred Tongues, from one to six,
> *(clap clap)* Phi weights them all — that's the golden trick!
> One, one-point-six, two-point-six, four-point-two,
> Six-point-eight, eleven — now the count is through!

**Math embedded:** The phi weights (1.000, 1.618, 2.618, 4.236, 6.854, 11.090) are spoken as rounded numbers in the final lines. Children learn the golden ratio progression through rhythm before they learn the math.

**Phonetic focus:** Each tongue's full name is spoken once per verse, embedding the pronunciation in a rhythmic context. The clapping provides a motor-memory anchor.

## Rhyme 2: Five Little Golems Jumping on the Bridge

*(Based on: Five Little Monkeys Jumping on the Bed)*

**Pattern: Subtraction from 5 to 0. Same structure each verse.**

> Five little golems stomping on the bridge,
> One fell off and rolled down the ridge.
> Clayborn called the Runic Engineer —
> Engineer said: *"No more golems stomping here!"*
>
> Four little golems stomping on the bridge,
> One fell off and cracked near the ridge.
> Clayborn called the Runic Engineer —
> Engineer said: *"No more golems stomping here!"*
>
> Three little golems stomping on the bridge...
> Two little golems stomping on the bridge...
> One little golem stomping on the bridge,
> He fell off and slid down the ridge.
> Clayborn called the Runic Engineer —
> Engineer said: *"NO. MORE. GOLEMS."*
>
> Zero golems stomping on the bridge.
> The bridge is fine. The ridge is fine.
> The Engineer went home for tea.
> And Clayborn swept the pieces, one through five.

**Math embedded:** Subtraction from 5 to 0. Each verse removes one golem. Children predict the count before it's said. The final verse introduces zero as a valid count — "zero golems" is a state, not an absence.

**AI lesson (light):** Repeated warnings ignored lead to escalating consequences. The engineer's patience degrades (like the signal detection lesson in Story 02).

## Rhyme 3: Hickory Dickory, Spiral Spire

*(Based on: Hickory Dickory Dock)*

**Pattern: Onomatopoeia + counting. Each verse adds one more chime.**

> Hickory dickory spire,
> The mouse ran up the wire.
> The Spire struck ONE — *(BONG)*
> The mouse was done,
> Hickory dickory spire.
>
> Hickory dickory spire,
> The cat chased after higher.
> The Spire struck TWO — *(BONG BONG)*
> And they both flew through,
> Hickory dickory spire.
>
> Hickory dickory spire,
> Polly watched the choir.
> The Spire struck THREE — *(BONG BONG BONG)*
> And the Academy's free,
> Hickory dickory spire.
>
> Hickory dickory spire,
> The students lit the fire.
> The Spire struck SIX — *(BONG x6)*
> Six tongues in the mix,
> Hickory dickory spire.
>
> Hickory dickory spire,
> The Spire struck FOURTEEN — *(BONG x14)*
> Fourteen layers, all serene,
> The pipeline hums, the pipeline's clean,
> Hickory dickory spire.

**Math embedded:** Counting by chimes. The jump from 3 to 6 (Sacred Tongues) and then to 14 (pipeline layers) teaches that numbers aren't always sequential — they cluster around meaningful values. Children learn 6 and 14 as significant numbers in the SCBE system.

**Phonetic focus:** "Hickory dickory" is pure phonetic play — the same sounds that make the original memorable make this version memorable, but now attached to Aethermoor concepts.

## Rhyme 4: The Phi Song

*(Based on: This Old Man — uses the same "knick-knack" refrain structure)*

**Pattern: Counting 0-5 with phi-power values. Each verse maps a phi exponent to a tongue.**

> Phi to zero, that is ONE,
> KO speaks and work is done.
> *(refrain)* With a knick-knack, golden stack,
> Give the tongue its power back!
> Phi is rolling, phi is fun!
>
> Phi to one is ONE-POINT-SIX,
> AV carries all the tricks.
> *(refrain)* With a knick-knack, golden stack...
>
> Phi to two is TWO-POINT-SIX,
> RU binds with heavy bricks.
> *(refrain)* With a knick-knack, golden stack...
>
> Phi to three is FOUR-POINT-TWO,
> CA computes for me and you.
> *(refrain)* With a knick-knack, golden stack...
>
> Phi to four is SIX-POINT-EIGHT,
> UM hides behind the gate.
> *(refrain)* With a knick-knack, golden stack...
>
> Phi to five, ELEVEN flies!
> DR stamps the proof and tries.
> *(refrain)* With a knick-knack, golden stack,
> Give the tongue its power back!
> All six tongues — the golden run!

**Math embedded:** Powers of phi: 1.000, 1.618, 2.618, 4.236, 6.854, 11.090 — rounded to one decimal for singability. Children learn exponential growth through melody. The fact that each weight is roughly 1.618 times the previous one can be discovered by a curious child who asks "why does it go 1, 1.6, 2.6, 4.2...?"

**Phonetic focus:** The "knick-knack golden stack" refrain is a phonetic anchor — same sounds every verse, creating expectation. The tongue names land on stressed syllables.

## Rhyme 5: Baa Baa Black Sheep of Viremont

*(Based on: Baa Baa Black Sheep)*

**Pattern: Question-and-answer. Counting bags/shares.**

> Baa baa, dream-sheep, floating in the air,
> Have you any wool from the Floating Meadows fair?
> Yes sir, yes sir, six bags full —
> One for Kor'aelin, to weave the commander's pull,
> One for Avali, to ship across the sea,
> One for Runethic, to bind what law must be,
> One for Cassisivadan, to calculate the thread,
> One for Umbroth, to hide what must not be said,
> One for Draumric, to stamp the final proof —
> Six Sacred Tongues beneath one roof!

**Math embedded:** Division — 6 bags distributed to 6 recipients. Each recipient gets exactly 1. Equal distribution. But the WEIGHTS are unequal (phi-powers), introducing the concept that equal share doesn't mean equal power. A more advanced child asks: "But if DR's bag is worth 11x more than KO's bag, is that fair?" The answer leads to a discussion of phi-weighted equity.

**Phonetic focus:** All six tongue names spoken clearly in sequence, each with a one-line function description. This is a MEMORY rhyme — designed to be recited until the tongue associations are automatic.

## Rhyme 6: Ring Around the Poincare

*(Based on: Ring Around the Rosie)*

**Pattern: Circle game with action. Children hold hands, walk in a circle, then "fall" at the end.**

> Ring around the Poincare,
> Pocket full of distance-play,
> Closer, closer, we're okay —
> We all stay SAFE!
>
> *(Children walk to center — d* decreasing)*
>
> Ring around the Poincare,
> Drifting out a little way,
> Amber lights begin to sway —
> QUARANTINE today!
>
> *(Children walk outward slowly)*
>
> Ring around the Poincare,
> Too far out, the runes all say,
> Costs a billion, cannot stay —
> We all fall DOWN!
>
> *(Children at the edge all sit/fall)*
>
> *(Then run back to center, laughing)*
> Back to center! Back to ALLOW!
> That's the safest place to be now!

**Math embedded:** The Poincare ball as a physical game. Walking to center = safe (d*=0). Walking to edge = dangerous (d*=3). The "fall" at the boundary is the super-exponential cost making operation impossible. Running back to center teaches that safety is a RETURN, not a restriction.

**Phonetic focus:** "Poincare" (PWAN-kah-RAY) is a real mathematician's name that sounds playful to children. Embedding it in a ring-game makes it memorable. "Distance-play" rhymes with "okay" and "sway" and "say" and "today" — the AY sound dominates, creating a singable pattern.

## Song Compilation: The Six Tongue Lullaby

*(Original — meant to be the bedtime song of Aethermoor)*

**Pattern: Slow, gentle, each verse introduces one tongue as a character tucking the child in.**

> KO whispers: "What do you want, little one?"
> *(Intent — the question before sleep)*
> AV carries: "Your dreams across the sun."
> *(Transport — dreams travel while you rest)*
> RU promises: "No nightmare breaks this ward."
> *(Governance — the rules that guard your sleep)*
> CA counts: "The stars outside — a billion, give or take."
> *(Compute — the math of wonder)*
> UM hides: "The monsters under your bed away."
> *(Security — concealment protects the vulnerable)*
> DR seals: "This night, this peace, this name, this day."
> *(Structure — the proof that morning will come)*
>
> Six tongues sing you safe to sleep,
> Six tongues guard the dreams you keep.
> Phi weights them all from light to deep —
> *One, one-point-six, two-point-six...*
> *(child falls asleep before the count finishes)*
> *...four... six... eleven...*
> *Shh.*

**Phonetic focus:** Soft consonants (sh, wh, s) throughout — designed to soothe. Each tongue's action (whispers, carries, promises, counts, hides, seals) is a gentle verb. The phi count at the end is meant to trail off as the child falls asleep — the math becomes a lullaby.
