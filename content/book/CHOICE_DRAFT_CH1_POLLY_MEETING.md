# Choice Draft: Polly's First Meeting — Branching Dialogue Test

## Status: DRAFT — Multiple variants for testing. Not yet in manuscript.

## Context
Marcus has just fallen through reality, hit stone, woken up in the crystal library.
He pushes himself up, looks around, and finds Polly.

## THE SCENE SETUP (shared across all branches)

Marcus got to his knees. His hands were shaking.

The crystal library stretched above him — shelves upon shelves, leather-bound volumes humming at frequencies he could feel in his molars. Sourceless light refracting through carved walls. The smell of old paper and ozone and something he had no name for.

He heard a page turn.

Not the hum of the books on the shelves. An actual page. Paper. The small, specific sound of someone reading.

He looked up.

A woman sat on the shelf nearest him, legs crossed, bare feet dangling four feet off the ground. She looked about twenty, though something in the way she held the book — one-handed, casual, the way you hold a book you've read eleven times — suggested that number was a polite fiction. Black feathers grew from her scalp instead of hair, cascading past her shoulders in an iridescent curtain. Wings folded against her back, dark as ink. Her eyes were polished obsidian — smooth, reflective, too bright.

She was reading a book with a pink cover.

She hadn't noticed him.

## BRANCH A: The Romantic Novel Version (Comedy + Character)

Marcus cleared his throat.

The woman startled — actually startled, which Marcus would later learn was something that happened to Polly approximately once per century — and in the space between one heartbeat and the next, she changed.

Not dramatically. Not with light or smoke. She *folded* — wings snapping in, body compressing, features sharpening into beak and talon with the panicked efficiency of someone minimizing a browser window when their boss walks in. The book tumbled from her hand and hit the stone floor with a slap that echoed through the library.

A raven sat on the shelf where the woman had been. Twice the size of any raven Marcus had ever seen, glossy black-to-violet feathers still settling from the hasty transformation. A miniature graduation cap materialized on her head at a jaunty angle. A monocle appeared over one eye. A bowtie knotted itself at her throat.

The book lay open on the floor between them.

Marcus looked down at it.

*The Flame and the Threshold: A Romance of the Second Realm*

The cover featured a shirtless elf leaning against a dimensional rift. Marcus could see the tagline: *"She was a boundary. He was determined to cross her."*

He looked up at the raven.

The raven looked at the book. Looked at Marcus. Looked at the book again.

"That," Polly said, with the precise dignity of someone who had been alive for five hundred years and was not about to be embarrassed by a mammal, "is research."

"Into what?"

"Interpersonal boundary dynamics."

"It has a shirtless elf on the cover."

"Elves have complex boundary systems. The shirtlessness is incidental." She ruffled her feathers — the full-body shake Marcus would later learn meant she was resettling something internal. "You're early."

"I'm— what?"

"Early. You weren't supposed to arrive until dawn. The Protocol's projection had you materializing in approximately—" she glanced at a crystal that Marcus hadn't noticed, embedded in the wall "—four hours and seventeen minutes. I was reading to pass the time."

"Interpersonal boundary dynamics."

"I will not be judged by someone who just fell through the floor of reality wearing a hoodie."

## BRANCH B: The Caught-Off-Guard Version (Vulnerability + Depth)

Marcus made a sound. Not a word — just air leaving his body in a way that announced the presence of a confused mammal.

The woman's head snapped up.

For one-quarter of a second, Marcus saw her face unguarded. Not the sarcasm he would come to know, not the five-hundred-year-old composure, not the practiced irritation of someone who had given this orientation speech before. Something younger. Something surprised. Something that looked, for that fractional instant, like a person who had been waiting alone for a very long time and had just been startled by the thing she was waiting for.

Then she shifted.

The transformation was fast — faster than the casual unfolding he would see later. This was emergency shapeshifting. Feathers erupting, bones compressing, the human form collapsing into raven like a tent folding in high wind. The book she'd been holding hit the floor. A graduation cap appeared. A monocle. A bowtie. Regalia assembled around her like armor.

The raven sat on the shelf and glared at him with the specific fury of someone who had been caught being a person instead of an institution.

"You," she said. "Are early."

Marcus was still on his knees. "Early for what?"

"Your dimensional transit was projected for dawn. The Protocol is usually precise. This is—" she clicked her beak once "—irregular."

Marcus looked at the book on the floor. A novel. Something with a worn spine and a soft cover. He couldn't read the title from this angle, but the edges of the pages were thumbed soft from rereading.

"Can I—"

"No." The word arrived before his hand reached the book. "That is personal material unrelated to your intake assessment."

"I was going to ask if I could stand up."

Polly's feathers tightened. Then loosened. The raven equivalent of someone who had just been slightly outmaneuvered and was recalibrating.

"Yes," she said. "You may stand. And then we will begin. I am Polly, Fifth Circle Keeper of the Archives, and you are Marcus Chen, and you are early, and I was not—" the smallest pause "—ready."

## BRANCH C: The Comedy-First Version (Banter Establishes Dynamic)

Marcus heard a page turn above him and looked up to find a woman sitting on a shelf reading a book with a cover so pink it was practically a declaration of war against good taste.

He stared at her. She hadn't noticed him.

She turned another page. Made a small sound — not quite a gasp, more a quiet "oh" — and bit her lower lip. Whatever she was reading, she was INTO it.

Marcus, who had just fallen through the floor of reality and was lying on cold stone in a crystal library in what was probably another dimension, did the only reasonable thing.

"Is it good?" he asked.

The woman screamed.

Well — not screamed exactly. Made a sound that started as a scream and ended as a caw, because somewhere in the middle of the sound she stopped being a woman and became a raven, feathers exploding outward like a startled cat in reverse, the book cartwheeling through the air and landing spine-up on the stone beside Marcus's elbow.

He picked it up.

*Heart of the Harmonic: When Two Frequencies Become One*

"So it IS a romance novel," Marcus said to the extremely large, extremely ruffled raven now perched above him wearing what appeared to be a graduation cap.

"It is a scholarly examination of resonance-based interpersonal dynamics," the raven said. "Put it down."

"The back cover says 'she never expected to fall for a maintenance engineer, but some frequencies can't be ignored.'"

"PUT IT DOWN."

Marcus set the book on the shelf. The raven's feathers were doing something complicated — flattening and rising in waves, like a sea that couldn't decide whether to be embarrassed or furious.

"I'm Marcus," he said. "I just fell through reality. Where am I?"

The raven took a breath. Adjusted her monocle. Straightened her graduation cap. Became, through sheer force of will, a professional.

"You're early," she said.

## TESTING PLAN

Send branches A, B, C, and D to the test reader panel as separate scenes.
Ask each model:
1. Which version makes you want to keep reading most?
2. Which Polly do you like best?
3. Which establishes the most about the world in the fewest words?
4. Which has the best single line?
5. Rank A, B, C, D.

Then use the winner (or a hybrid of the top 2) in the manuscript.
