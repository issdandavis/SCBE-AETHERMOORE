# Patent Moot Court Case Study
## SCBE-2026-0001 / U.S. Provisional Application No. 63/961,403
## Title: System and Method for Hyperbolic Geometry-Based Authorization with Topological Control-Flow Integrity

Status: internal drafting workshop, not legal advice.

## Purpose

This document uses SCBE-AETHERMOORE as the case study for an adversarial patent
drafting workshop. The point is to patent as much of the system as legally and
realistically supportable without weakening the application through unsupported
claims, vague language, or overbroad abstractions.

The system matters more than any single phrase. The workshop therefore separates:

- the client's interest: broad protection around the valuable system;
- the invention's interest: accurate technical disclosure and enabled claims;
- the examiner's interest: rejecting abstract, obvious, unsupported, or unclear
  claims;
- the drafter's job: convert the debate into claim families, specification
  support, figures, and fallback positions.

## Cast

### Client Advocate

Represents the inventor's business interest. This side pushes for the broadest
coverage that can reasonably be supported:

- govern AI, agentic tools, CLIs, APIs, buses, and distributed workflows;
- cover hyperbolic authorization, semantic/tongue weighting, quarantine,
  tamper/canonicality checks, audit receipts, topological control-flow
  integrity, and runtime policy enforcement;
- preserve room for continuations and future product variants;
- avoid accidentally donating valuable system structure to the public domain.

### Invention Advocate

Represents the technical system itself. This side resists hype and asks what is
actually implemented, disclosed, and reproducible:

- do the claims match the filed provisional and current code;
- are terms defined in neutral engineering language;
- can a skilled engineer build the system from the specification;
- is the claim language tied to concrete data structures, transforms,
  thresholds, decisions, receipts, and runtime behavior;
- are new features separated from priority-supported features.

### Examiner Skeptic

Represents the rejection pressure:

- Section 101: is this just an abstract rule, math formula, or policy decision;
- Section 102: did prior art already disclose the same combination;
- Section 103: would combining known embeddings, anomaly detection, access
  control, and cryptographic receipts be obvious;
- Section 112: are the claims enabled, definite, and supported;
- clarity: are coined terms necessary or are they hiding ordinary mechanisms.

### Drafting Judge

Turns the argument into usable drafting decisions:

- keep broad claims anchored to concrete computer operations;
- define coined terms in the specification, but claim standard mechanisms;
- include fallback dependent claims for each subsystem;
- keep unsupported future work out of priority-dependent claims;
- mark continuation/CIP candidates instead of forcing them into the first
  non-provisional.

## Round 1: What Is The Invention?

### Client Advocate

SCBE is not merely a classifier. It is a governed execution substrate. An input
action, request, command, or agent event is mapped into a geometric state, scored
against trusted behavior, and routed into allow, review, quarantine, deny, or
receipt-producing execution paths. The valuable thing is the whole stack: the
gate, the route, the audit, the containment state, and the cross-language CLI/API
surface.

The first independent claim should not be so narrow that a competitor can remove
one named tongue, swap the exact metric, or run the same concept in a different
agent harness and avoid coverage.

### Invention Advocate

The broadest defensible invention is a computer-implemented governance method
using a nonlinear geometric distance/cost measure over encoded action context to
control execution. The specification can describe the six tongues, golden-ratio
weights, Poincare ball, harmonic wall, topological CFI, tamper detection, and
quarantine as embodiments and dependent claims.

Do not make the independent claim depend on every poetic or project-specific
term. The strongest broad claim should survive even if "Sacred Tongues" is
renamed to "semantic weighting axes."

### Examiner Skeptic

If the claim says "measure semantic drift and deny bad requests," it risks being
an abstract idea. If it says "use hyperbolic geometry for authorization," it may
still look like math. The claim needs a concrete technological improvement:
controlling execution of computational actions through a specific encoded state,
nonlinear cost, configured thresholds, containment decisions, and audit receipts.

### Drafting Judge

Claim the invention as a runtime control mechanism, not as a theory:

1. receive an action/context;
2. encode it into a vector/state;
3. embed or project it into a nonlinear geometric domain;
4. compare it against a trusted/session/reference state;
5. compute a governance cost or distance;
6. combine that with one or more integrity/tamper/coherence signals;
7. select an execution state;
8. enforce that state in the runtime;
9. generate an audit receipt.

## Round 2: How Broad Can We Go?

### Client Advocate

The patent should cover:

- AI prompt governance;
- command-line agent governance;
- API request governance;
- multi-agent bus governance;
- file/code patch governance;
- topological control-flow integrity;
- quarantine locks and token-drain containment;
- semantic/tongue weighting;
- bijective tokenizer/canonicality tamper signals;
- post-quantum audit receipts.

### Invention Advocate

Those can be covered as families, but not all at the same claim level. The
non-provisional should use layered claims:

- broad independent method claim for geometric authorization;
- independent system claim for runtime architecture;
- independent computer-readable medium claim for tamper-aware governance;
- dependent claims for each subsystem;
- continuation candidates for later fleet, board/topology, workflow, and
  model-harness claims if the provisional does not fully support them.

### Examiner Skeptic

Packing every subsystem into one independent claim narrows it and creates more
ways to reject it. Claiming all future versions risks unsupported matter. Also,
cryptographic receipts and PQC may be known; their novelty may be in the
combination with governance decisions, not the primitives alone.

### Drafting Judge

Use a claim tree instead of one overloaded mega-claim.

## Round 3: The 101 Attack

### Examiner Skeptic

This looks like math and policy: encode, calculate distance, decide allow or
deny. Why is this not an abstract idea implemented on a generic computer?

### Client Advocate

Because the claims are directed to controlling machine execution, not merely
displaying a risk score. The system changes runtime behavior: blocks commands,
quarantines actions, seals receipts, routes tool execution, and changes what an
agent or CLI is allowed to do.

### Invention Advocate

The specification should emphasize the technical problem:

- LLM and agent tools can execute unsafe or tampered commands;
- ordinary classifiers and blocklists are brittle;
- sequential agent workflows need persistent execution-state governance;
- the invention enforces runtime state transitions using geometric drift and
  integrity signals.

The claims should recite enforcement steps and data structures, not just
"analyzing meaning."

### Drafting Judge

Add language to claims and specification showing a practical application:

- "controlling execution of the computational action";
- "placing the action in a quarantine containment state";
- "preventing dispatch to a tool runner";
- "generating an audit receipt identifying the decision state";
- "updating a session centroid or trusted reference state";
- "persisting runtime gate state across evaluations."

## Round 4: The 102/103 Attack

### Examiner Skeptic

Prior art includes anomaly detection, embeddings, hyperbolic embeddings,
zero-trust access control, policy engines, semantic filters, control-flow
integrity, and cryptographic audit logs. Combining them may be obvious.

### Client Advocate

The value is the combination and the execution path:

- hyperbolic drift measurement applied to action authorization;
- semantic weighting axes changing governance cost;
- topological control-flow integrity tied to runtime decision states;
- tamper/canonicality checks as part of the same gate;
- quarantine/deny/allow routing with audit receipts.

### Invention Advocate

The prior-art search must be brutally honest. For every known component, the
distinction should be:

- not "nobody ever used embeddings";
- not "nobody ever used access control";
- but "the prior art does not disclose this ordered combination for governed
  execution of AI/tool actions under nonlinear geometric cost and integrity
  gating."

### Drafting Judge

Build the prior-art log around combinations, not isolated keywords. Search for:

- hyperbolic embedding access control;
- geometric anomaly detection authorization;
- LLM tool-use policy gate;
- semantic drift command authorization;
- control-flow integrity AI agents;
- cryptographic audit receipt policy engine;
- quarantine state agent execution.

## Round 5: The 112 Attack

### Examiner Skeptic

Terms like "Sacred Tongues," "harmonic wall," "GeoSeal," and "Spiralverse" may
be indefinite if they are used as claim requirements without definition.

### Client Advocate

Those terms are part of the product identity and origin. They should not be
erased.

### Invention Advocate

Keep them in definitions, examples, and figures. In claims, pair each coined
term with a standard technical description:

- Sacred Tongues -> semantic weighting axes;
- Harmonic Wall -> nonlinear governance cost function;
- GeoSeal -> governance gate and audit receipt mechanism;
- Spiralverse -> historical/project-origin context, not a required claim term.

### Drafting Judge

Use a terminology decoder in the specification. Claims should stand even if a
reader ignores the lore names.

## Narrative Exercise: Law Students In The Workshop

The students divide the whiteboard into two columns. On the left, they write
"protect the client." On the right, they write "protect the invention." The
client side wants a wall around the whole system: AI prompts, shell commands,
agent buses, quarantine locks, tamper signals, receipts, and future workflow
boards. The invention side keeps circling phrases and asking, "Where is the
support? Where is the figure? Where is the code path?"

The examiner group waits until both sides are confident, then attacks. They say
the words "hyperbolic geometry" do not save an abstract claim. They say
embeddings are known, policy gates are known, audit logs are known, and control
flow integrity is known. The room gets quieter. The answer is not to get louder.
The answer is to write the system more precisely.

The drafting judge rewrites the board as a machine path:

an action enters;
the action becomes an encoded state;
the encoded state enters a geometric domain;
the system compares it to a trusted trajectory;
semantic weights and integrity signals modify the cost;
the runtime selects a governed state;
the tool runner is allowed, quarantined, denied, or escalated;
the result is sealed in a receipt;
the persisted gate state updates for the next action.

That path is the invention spine. Everything else becomes a dependent branch,
an embodiment, a figure, or a continuation candidate.

## Drafting Rules From The Debate

1. Claim the execution-control path before claiming philosophy.
2. Use standard technical terms in claims and SCBE terms in definitions.
3. Keep independent claims broad but concrete.
4. Use dependent claims to capture the richest SCBE details.
5. Separate priority-supported features from newer continuation material.
6. Treat every claim element as guilty until it has support in the spec, code,
   figure, or filed provisional.
7. Do not claim "all AI governance"; claim the ordered SCBE mechanism.
8. Do not rely on "post-quantum" alone for novelty; use it as receipt/security
   integration.
9. Do not hide weak support behind coined terms.
10. Let the adversarial review improve the claims before the examiner ever sees
    them.
