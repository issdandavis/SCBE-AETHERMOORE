# Ternary Balance and Critical Line Notes

**Date:** 2026-03-25
**Lane:** theories-untested
**Status:** Speculative structural analogy, not a proof

---

## Core Thought

The critical line is not being framed here as a binary midpoint.

It is being framed as a **ternary balance point**.

Working intuition:

- binary split: two states
- ternary split: three states
- the neutral state forces a center

Balanced ternary:

```text
+1, 0, -1
```

The zero is the balance point because it is the only position symmetric with respect to the two opposites.

Mapped onto the interval `[0,1]`, the center of symmetry becomes:

```text
1/2
```

So the research question becomes:

```text
What if the critical line is at 1/2 because the underlying structure is ternary, not binary?
```

---

## Structural Claim Being Explored

Not:

- the zeros happen to lie on `Re(s) = 1/2`

But:

- if the relevant structure is inherently ternary
- and ternary balance forces a unique center
- then the critical line may be the forced center of that structure

This is a structural analogy, not a theorem.

## Plateau Analogy

Useful analogy:

- three soap films meeting symmetrically force `120°`
- not by arbitrary choice
- but by the structure of the equilibrium

By analogy:

- three symbolic states meeting symmetrically may force a unique balance point
- and on the interval `[0,1]` that balance point is `1/2`

This is the strongest metaphorical form of the note:

```text
The critical line may be the Plateau border of number theory.
```

Again:

- useful analogy
- not proof

## Safer Mathematical Restatement

The defensible form is:

1. define a ternary system with states `-1, 0, +1`
2. define its symmetry center
3. map that center into the chosen interval or coordinate system
4. ask whether the target structure behaves like a fixed-point set of that ternary symmetry

That is a legitimate research question.

What is **not** legitimate yet:

- saying this proves Riemann
- saying the zeta function is known to be ternary in this sense
- saying the line is forced without formal derivation
