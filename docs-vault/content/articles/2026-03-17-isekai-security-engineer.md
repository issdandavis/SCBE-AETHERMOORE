# My MC is a security engineer who falls into a world governed by six programming languages disguised as magic

**Posted to r/litrpg (or r/isekai)**

---

Marcus Chen is 32, Asian American, runs on bad coffee and worse sleep, and has spent his entire career arguing with authentication systems. He works security at a San Francisco tech company. He has never once believed in magic. His idea of a spiritual experience is a clean CI/CD pipeline.

Then the floor opens and he falls into Aethermoor.

Aethermoor runs on six sacred languages -- the tongues -- and every single one of them maps to something Marcus already knows, just sideways. Kor'aelin is intent declaration. Avali is transport protocol. Runethic is policy enforcement. Cassisivadan is compute. Umbroth is security. Draumric is schema and authentication.

He's standing in a crystal corridor that exists in six places at once, where doorways appear when you approach and vanish when you pass, and the hum in the walls sounds like a server room, and the first thing he does -- the very first thing -- is start pattern-matching. Not panicking. Pattern-matching. Because Marcus trusts frameworks faster than he trusts people, and this place has the unmistakable smell of a system.

His guide is a raven the size of a border collie named Polly. Full title: Polydimensional Manifestation of Accumulated Wisdom and Occasional Sarcasm. She wears a tiny graduation cap and a monocle and she talks like a tenured professor who has been forced to tutor a freshman. She is not patient. She is not kind. She is, however, brutally honest and centuries old and the only thing standing between Marcus and the thing that's actually going to kill him.

The thing that's going to kill him is the Protocol itself.

Aethermoor has a heartbeat. Every 0.3 seconds, a pulse passes through the entire world -- every crystal, every root, every floating island. If you belong, it feels like a low hum in your chest. If you don't belong, the pulse notices. And Marcus doesn't belong. He's an unregistered entity in a system that does not tolerate unregistered entities.

He has 72 hours to be verified or the world will simply... remove him. Not dramatically. Not with lightning or monsters. The ground will stop supporting his weight. The air will stop entering his lungs. The tongues will stop resolving his words. He'll dissolve into the substrate like a process getting killed by the OS.

So Marcus has to learn a magic system that is, functionally, a six-dimensional trust protocol -- weighted by the golden ratio, enforced by hyperbolic geometry, and governed by people who have been running this infrastructure for centuries and are not thrilled about an unscheduled arrival. There's a woman named Senna Thorne whose competence is so tightly held it's visible, who adjusts her right cuff when she's deciding whether to trust you. There's a maintenance lead named Bram who treats the world's infrastructure like a living engine and speaks in three-word sentences. There's a policy adjudicator with silver hair braided with literal policy tags.

And there's Kael. A teenager with a grandfather's patience and code signatures that are clean, recursive, and self-propagating. He was right about a vulnerability in the system at fourteen. He weaponized it by sixteen. He's the reason the Harmonic Wall has scars.

Marcus is not the chosen one. He's not special. He's a tired engineer with keyboard hands and dark circles under his eyes who keeps reaching for a coffee mug that isn't there. What he has is a particular way of looking at impossible things -- like they're bugs with unusually good UI.

That turns out to be exactly what Aethermoor needs. Because something is wrong with the Protocol, and the people who've been running it for centuries are too close to see it. It takes an outsider. It takes someone who looks at a sacred language and sees a routing table.

It takes a guy who argues with authentication systems for a living.

*The Six Tongues Protocol* is on Kindle. If you've ever wanted an isekai where the MC's cheat skill is "thinks like a sysadmin," this is it.
